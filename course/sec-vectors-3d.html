    Section 1.2 Vectors in Three Dimensions

    In order to represent points in space, we first choose a fixed point \(O\) (the origin) and three directed lines through \(O\) which are perpendicular to each other, called the coordinate axes. They are labeled as the \(x\)-axis, \(y\)-axis, and \(z\)-axis. Think of the \(x\)- and \(y\)-axes as being horizontal while the \(z\)-axis is vertical. The direction of the \(z\)-axis is determined by the right-hand rule.
    🔗
    The three coordinate axes determine the three coordinate planes. The \(xy\)-plane contains the \(x\)- and \(y\)-axes, the \(yz\)-plane contains the \(y\)- and \(z\)-axes, and the \(xz\)-plane contains the \(x\)- and \(z\)-axes. These three coordinate planes divide space into eight parts, called octants. The first octant is determined by the positive axes.
    🔗
    We represent a point \(P\) by the ordered triple \((a,b,c)\) of real numbers and we call \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\) the coordinates of \(P\text{;}\) here, \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\) are the \(x\)-, \(y\)-, and \(z\)-coordinates, respectively.
    🔗
    The point \(P(a,b,c)\) can be visualized as a corner of a rectangular box. If we drop a perpendicular from \(P\) to the \(xy\)-plane, we get a point \(Q\) with coordinates \((a,b,0)\) called the projection of \(P\) on the \(xy\)-plane.
    🔗
    The Cartesian product \(\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R} = \{(x,y,z) \mid x,y,z \in \mathbb{R}\}\) is the set of all ordered triples of real numbers and is denoted by \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 1.25.

    What surfaces in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) are represented by the following equations?
    🔗
    1. \(\displaystyle z=3\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle y=5\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. The equation \(z=3\) represents the set of all points \(\{(x,y,z) \mid z=3\}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{.}\) Since \(x\) and \(y\) can be any real numbers, this forms a horizontal plane parallel to the \(xy\)-plane, located exactly \(3\) units above it.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The equation \(y=5\) represents the set of all points \(\{(x,y,z) \mid y=5\}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{.}\) Since \(x\) and \(z\) can be any real numbers, this forms a vertical plane parallel to the \(xz\)-plane, located exactly \(5\) units to the right of it.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Fact 1.28. Distance Formula in Three Dimensions.

    The distance \(d(P_1, P_2) = |P_1P_2|\) between the points \(P_1(x_1,y_1,z_1)\) and \(P_2(x_2,y_2,z_2)\) is:
    \begin{equation*} d(P_1, P_2) = |P_1P_2| = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2 + (z_2-z_1)^2} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.29.

    Find the distance from the point \(P(-1,-3,1)\) to the point \(Q(3,4,-2)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Substitute the coordinates of \(P\) and \(Q\) into the three-dimensional distance formula:
    \begin{align*} |PQ| \amp = \sqrt{(3 - (-1))^2 + (4 - (-3))^2 + (-2 - 1)^2}\\ \amp = \sqrt{(3 + 1)^2 + (4 + 3)^2 + (-3)^2}\\ \amp = \sqrt{4^2 + 7^2 + (-3)^2}\\ \amp = \sqrt{16 + 49 + 9}\\ \amp = \sqrt{74} \end{align*}
    The exact distance between the points is \(\sqrt{74}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Fact 1.30. Equation of a Sphere.

    An equation of a sphere with center \(C(h,k,l)\) and radius \(r\) is:
    \begin{equation*} (x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 + (z-l)^2 = r^2 \end{equation*}
    If the center is at the origin \(O(0,0,0)\text{,}\) then the equation of the sphere simplifies to:
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = r^2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.31.

    Show that \(x^2+y^2+z^2+4x-6y+2z+6=0\) is the equation of a sphere, and find its center and radius.
    🔗
    Solution.
    To transform the given equation into standard form, group the variables and complete the square for each variable:
    \begin{align*} (x^2 + 4x) + (y^2 - 6y) + (z^2 + 2z) \amp = -6\\ \left(x^2 + 4x + \left(\frac{4}{2}\right)^2\right) + \left(y^2 - 6y + \left(\frac{-6}{2}\right)^2\right) + \left(z^2 + 2z + \left(\frac{2}{2}\right)^2\right) \amp = -6 + 4 + 9 + 1\\ (x^2 + 4x + 4) + (y^2 - 6y + 9) + (z^2 + 2z + 1) \amp = 8\\ (x + 2)^2 + (y - 3)^2 + (z + 1)^2 \amp = 8 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Comparing this result to the standard equation of a sphere \((x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 + (z-l)^2 = r^2\text{,}\) we find:
    • The center of the sphere is \(C(h,k,l) = C(-2, 3, -1)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The radius is \(r = \sqrt{8} = 2\sqrt{2}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.32.

    What region in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) is represented by the following inequalities?
    \begin{equation*} 1 \leq x^2+y^2+z^2 \leq 4 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    The expression \(x^2+y^2+z^2 = r^2\) represents the squared distance from the origin to a point in three-dimensional space. We can rewrite the inequality by taking the square root of all parts:
    \begin{equation*} 1 \leq \sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2} \leq 2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This represents the set of all points whose distance \(r\) from the origin satisfies \(1 \leq r \leq 2\text{.}\) Therefore, the region is a solid spherical shell (or a thick hollow sphere) centered at the origin, including the inner sphere of radius \(1\) and the outer sphere of radius \(2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.34.

    What region in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) is represented by the following inequalities?
    \begin{equation*} x^2+y^2 \leq 25, \quad |z| \leq 3 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    We analyze the two conditions independently to find their intersection:
    • In the \(xy\)-plane, \(x^2+y^2 \leq 25\) describes a solid disk of radius \(5\) centered at the origin. Since \(z\) is unconstrained by this expression, it forms an infinitely long solid cylinder extending along the \(z\)-axis.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The absolute value inequality \(|z| \leq 3\) is equivalent to \(-3 \leq z \leq 3\text{.}\) This bounds the space vertically between two horizontal planes.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Combining these conditions restricts the infinite cylinder to a specific height. Therefore, the region is a solid vertical cylinder of radius \(5\) and height \(6\text{,}\) centered along the \(z\)-axis and extending from \(z = -3\) to \(z = 3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Fact 1.36. Vector Operations in Three Dimensions.

    Let \(\vec{a} = \langle a_1,a_2,a_3 \rangle\) and \(\vec{b} = \langle b_1,b_2,b_3 \rangle\) be vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Addition of vectors:
      \begin{equation*} \vec{a}+\vec{b} = \langle a_1+b_1, a_2+b_2, a_3+b_3 \rangle \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Scalar multiple of a vector:
      \begin{equation*} c\vec{a} = c\langle a_1,a_2,a_3 \rangle = \langle ca_1, ca_2, ca_3 \rangle \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The zero vector 0:
      \begin{equation*} \vec{0} = \langle 0,0,0 \rangle \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    4. The negative -a of a vector \(\vec{a}\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} -\vec{a} = -\langle a_1,a_2,a_3 \rangle = \langle -a_1, -a_2, -a_3 \rangle \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    5. The difference a-b:
      \begin{equation*} \vec{a}-\vec{b} = \vec{a}+(-\vec{b}) = \langle a_1-b_1, a_2-b_2, a_3-b_3 \rangle \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    6. The length of \(\vec{a} = \langle a_1,a_2,a_3 \rangle\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \|\vec{a}\| = \sqrt{a_1^2+a_2^2+a_3^2} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    7. If \(P_1(x_1,y_1,z_1)\) and \(P_2(x_2,y_2,z_2)\) are any points, the vector \(\vec{a}\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) that corresponds to \(\vec{P_1P_2}\) is:
      \begin{equation*} \vec{a} = \langle x_2-x_1, y_2-y_1, z_2-z_1 \rangle \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.37.

    The standard basis vectors in three dimensions are:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{i} = \langle 1,0,0 \rangle, \quad \vec{j} = \langle 0,1,0 \rangle, \quad \vec{k} = \langle 0,0,1 \rangle \end{equation*}
    If \(\vec{a} = \langle a_1,a_2,a_3 \rangle\text{,}\) then it can be written as a linear combination of the standard unit basis vectors:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} = a_1\vec{i} + a_2\vec{j} + a_3\vec{k} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.38.

    If \(\vec{a} = \vec{i} + 2\vec{j} - 3\vec{k}\) and \(\vec{b} = 4\vec{i} + 7\vec{k}\text{,}\) express the vector \(2\vec{a} + 3\vec{b}\) in terms of \(\vec{i}\text{,}\) \(\vec{j}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{k}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Substitute the given expressions for \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\text{,}\) distribute the scalars, and group the components:
    \begin{align*} 2\vec{a} + 3\vec{b} \amp = 2(\vec{i} + 2\vec{j} - 3\vec{k}) + 3(4\vec{i} + 0\vec{j} + 7\vec{k})\\ \amp = (2\vec{i} + 4\vec{j} - 6\vec{k}) + (12\vec{i} + 0\vec{j} + 21\vec{k})\\ \amp = (2 + 12)\vec{i} + (4 + 0)\vec{j} + (-6 + 21)\vec{k}\\ \amp = 14\vec{i} + 4\vec{j} + 15\vec{k} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.39.

    Find the unit vector in the direction of the vector \(\vec{v} = 2\vec{i} - \vec{j} - 2\vec{k}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, find the magnitude of the given vector \(\vec{v}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \|\vec{v}\| = \sqrt{2^2 + (-1)^2 + (-2)^2} = \sqrt{4 + 1 + 4} = \sqrt{9} = 3 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Next, multiply the reciprocal of the magnitude by the vector \(\vec{v}\) to produce the unit vector \(\vec{u}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \vec{u} = \frac{1}{3}(2\vec{i} - \vec{j} - 2\vec{k}) = \frac{2}{3}\vec{i} - \frac{1}{3}\vec{j} - \frac{2}{3}\vec{k} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.40.

    A 100-lb weight hangs from two wires making angles of \(50^\circ\) and \(32^\circ\) with the horizontal. Find the tensions (forces) \(\vec{T}_1\) and \(\vec{T}_2\) in both wires and their magnitudes.
    🔗
    Solution.
    We express the tension vectors in terms of their horizontal and vertical components. Let \(\vec{T}_1\) pull up and to the left, and \(\vec{T}_2\) pull up and to the right:
    \begin{align*} \vec{T}_1 \amp = -\|\vec{T}_1\|\cos(50^\circ)\vec{i} + \|\vec{T}_1\|\sin(50^\circ)\vec{j}\\ \vec{T}_2 \amp = \|\vec{T}_2\|\cos(32^\circ)\vec{i} + \|\vec{T}_2\|\sin(32^\circ)\vec{j} \end{align*}
    🔗
    The downward force due to the weight is \(\vec{w} = -100\vec{j}\text{.}\) For the system to achieve static equilibrium, the resultant sum of the tensions must exactly counterbalance the weight:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{T}_1 + \vec{T}_2 = -\vec{w} = 100\vec{j} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Equating the horizontal (\(\vec{i}\)) and vertical (\(\vec{j}\)) components gives a system of linear equations:
    \begin{align*} -\|\vec{T}_1\|\cos(50^\circ) + \|\vec{T}_2\|\cos(32^\circ) \amp = 0\\ \|\vec{T}_1\|\sin(50^\circ) + \|\vec{T}_2\|\sin(32^\circ) \amp = 100 \end{align*}
    🔗
    From the first equation, we find \(\|\vec{T}_2\| = \|\vec{T}_1\|\frac{\cos(50^\circ)}{\cos(32^\circ)}\text{.}\) Substituting this relationship into the second equation yields:
    \begin{equation*} \|\vec{T}_1\|\sin(50^\circ) + \|\vec{T}_1\|\frac{\cos(50^\circ)\sin(32^\circ)}{\cos(32^\circ)} = 100 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \|\vec{T}_1\|\left(\sin(50^\circ) + \cos(50^\circ)\tan(32^\circ)\right) = 100 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solving for the magnitudes yields:
    • \(\displaystyle \|\vec{T}_1\| \approx 85.64\text{ lb}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle \|\vec{T}_2\| = 85.64 \cdot \frac{\cos(50^\circ)}{\cos(32^\circ)} \approx 64.91\text{ lb}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Substituting these scalar magnitudes back into our component formulas provides the final tension vector models:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{T}_1 \approx -55.05\vec{i} + 65.60\vec{j} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \vec{T}_2 \approx 55.05\vec{i} + 34.40\vec{j} \end{equation*}
