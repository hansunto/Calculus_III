    Section 4.6 Change of Variables and Jacobians

    In this section, we will explore the technique of changing variables in multiple integrals. This method allows us to simplify the evaluation of integrals by transforming the region of integration into a more convenient shape.
    🔗
    In one-dimensional calculus, we often use a change of variables (a substitution) to simplify an integral as:
    \begin{equation*} \int^b_a f(x)\,dx = \int^d_c f(g(u))g'(u)\,du, \end{equation*}
    where \(x=g(u)\text{,}\) \(a=g(c)\text{,}\) and \(b=g(d)\text{.}\) We consider a change of variables that is given by a transformation \(T\) from the \(uv\)-plane to the \(xy\)-plane:
    \begin{equation*} T(u,v) = (x,y), \end{equation*}
    where \(x\) and \(y\) are related to \(u\) and \(v\) by the equations:
    \begin{equation*} x=g(u,v), \quad y=h(u,v). \end{equation*}
    A transformation \(T\) is a function whose domain and range are both subsets of \(\mathbb{R}^2\text{.}\) If \(T(u_1,v_1)=(x_1,y_1)\text{,}\) then the point \((x_1,y_1)\) is called the image of the point \((u_1,v_1)\text{.}\) If no two points share the same image, \(T\) is called one-to-one. If \(T\) is a one-to-one transformation, then it has an inverse transformation \(T^{-1}\) from the \(xy\)-plane to the \(uv\)-plane, making it possible to solve the equations for \(u\) and \(v\) in terms of \(x\) and \(y\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} u=G(x,y), \quad v=H(x,y). \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 4.71.

    A transformation \(T\) is defined by the equations:
    \begin{equation*} x = u^2 - v^2, \quad y = 2uv. \end{equation*}
    Find the image of the square region \(S = \{(u,v): 0 \leq u \leq 1, \ 0 \leq v \leq 1\}\) under this transformation.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Step 1: Map the Four Boundaries of the Square \(S\)
    🔗
    To find the image region \(R\) in the \(xy\)-plane, we map the four boundary edges of the square \(S\) in the \(uv\)-plane one by one:
    🔗
    • Boundary \(S_1\) (Bottom Edge): Along this line, \(v = 0\) while \(u\) runs from \(0\) to \(1\text{.}\) Substituting \(v = 0\) into our transformation equations gives:
      \begin{equation*} x = u^2, \quad y = 0. \end{equation*}
      As \(u\) ranges from \(0\) to \(1\text{,}\) \(x\) moves from \(0\) to \(1\text{.}\) This maps to a straight line segment along the positive \(x\)-axis from \((0,0)\) to \((1,0)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Boundary \(S_2\) (Right Edge): Along this line, \(u = 1\) while \(v\) runs from \(0\) to \(1\text{.}\) Substituting \(u = 1\) yields:
      \begin{equation*} x = 1 - v^2, \quad y = 2v. \end{equation*}
      We can eliminate the parameter \(v\) by isolating it from the second equation: \(v = \frac{y}{2}\text{.}\) Substituting this into the first equation describes a parabola in the \(xy\)-plane:
      \begin{equation*} x = 1 - \left(\frac{y}{2}\right)^2 \implies x = 1 - \frac{1}{4}y^2. \end{equation*}
      As \(v\) moves from \(0\) to \(1\text{,}\) \(y\) sweeps from \(0\) to \(2\text{,}\) mapping this boundary to a parabolic arc running from \((1,0)\) up to \((0,2)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Boundary \(S_3\) (Top Edge): Along this line, \(v = 1\) while \(u\) runs from \(1\) down to \(0\text{.}\) Substituting \(v = 1\) gives:
      \begin{equation*} x = u^2 - 1, \quad y = 2u \implies u = \frac{y}{2}. \end{equation*}
      Substituting \(u\) back into the expression for \(x\) describes another parabola:
      \begin{equation*} x = \left(\frac{y}{2}\right)^2 - 1 \implies x = \frac{1}{4}y^2 - 1. \end{equation*}
      As \(u\) moves from \(1\) down to \(0\text{,}\) \(y\) tracks from \(2\) down to \(0\text{,}\) mapping this boundary to a parabolic arc from \((0,2)\) back left to \((-1,0)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Boundary \(S_4\) (Left Edge): Along this line, \(u = 0\) while \(v\) tracks from \(1\) down to \(0\text{.}\) Substituting \(u = 0\) gives:
      \begin{equation*} x = -v^2, \quad y = 0. \end{equation*}
      As \(v\) ranges from \(1\) down to \(0\text{,}\) \(x\) moves from \(-1\) back to \(0\text{.}\) This maps directly to a straight line segment along the negative \(x\)-axis from \((-1,0)\) returning to the origin \((0,0)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Step 2: Conclusion
    🔗
    The image of the square region \(S\) is the upper lens-shaped region \(R\) in the upper half-plane of the \(xy\)-space bounded below by the interval \([-1, 1]\) along the \(x\)-axis and capped above by the two meeting parabolas intersecting at \((0,2)\text{.}\)

    Example 4.74.

    If \(P(x,y)\) is any point with \(x \neq 0\) in the \(xy\)-plane, let:
    \begin{equation*} r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}, \quad \theta = \arctan\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) \end{equation*}
    and let \(P(0,b)\) correspond to \(r=b, \ \theta =\pi/2\text{.}\) Sketch in the \(r\theta\)-plane, the graphs of \(r=1, \ r=2, \ r=3\) and \(\theta=-1/2, \ \theta =1/2\text{.}\) Sketch the corresponding \(r\)-curves and \(\theta\)-curves in the \(xy\)-plane.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Analysis and Interpretation:
    🔗
    This problem demonstrates how grid curves translate between a polar parameter coordinate space (\(r\theta\)-plane) and physical space (\(xy\)-plane):
    🔗
    • In the \(r\theta\)-plane, constant-radius curves (\(r=c\)) are simple vertical lines, and constant-angle curves (\(\theta=c\)) are simple horizontal lines. Together, they form a orthogonal rectangular grid network.
      🔗
      🔗
    • In the \(xy\)-plane, constant-radius curves (\(r=c\)) transform into concentric circles centered at the origin with a radius of \(c\text{.}\) Constant-angle curves (\(\theta=c\)) transform into straight radial rays shooting outward from the origin at a baseline heading angle of \(c\) radians (where \(\pm 1/2 \text{ rad} \approx \pm 28.6^\circ\)).
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 4.77. The Jacobian Determinant.

    If \(x=f(u,v)\) and \(y=g(u,v)\text{,}\) then the Jacobian of \(x\) and \(y\) with respect to \(u\) and \(v\text{,}\) denoted by \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial (u,v)}\text{,}\) is:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial (u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} \amp \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} \amp \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{vmatrix} = \frac{\partial x}{\partial u}\frac{\partial y}{\partial v} - \frac{\partial y}{\partial u}\frac{\partial x}{\partial v}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 4.78. Change of Variables in a Double Integral.

    If \(x=f(u,v)\) and \(y=g(u,v)\) is a transformation of coordinates, and if the Jacobian \(\displaystyle \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial (u,v)}\) does not change sign in \(S\text{,}\) then:
    \begin{equation*} \iint_R F(x,y)\,dA = \iint_S F(f(u,v), g(u,v)) \left| \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial (u,v)} \right| \,dudv. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.79.

    Evaluate the double integral:
    \begin{equation*} \int^4_0 \int^{x=(y/2)+1}_{x=y/2} \frac{2x-y}{2} \, dxdy \end{equation*}
    by applying the transformation:
    \begin{equation*} u = \frac{2x-y}{2}, \quad v = \frac{y}{2} \end{equation*}
    and integrating over the corresponding region in the \(uv\)-plane.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Step 1: Find the Inverse Transformation and the Jacobian
    🔗
    To transition the integral into the new coordinate system, we solve the transformation equations for \(x\) and \(y\) in terms of \(u\) and \(v\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} v = \frac{y}{2} \implies y = 2v \end{equation*}
    Substituting this into the first equation:
    \begin{equation*} u = \frac{2x - 2v}{2} \implies u = x - v \implies x = u + v \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Next, we calculate the Jacobian determinant \(J(u,v)\) of this transformation:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} \amp \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} \amp \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 2 \end{vmatrix} = (1)(2) - (1)(0) = 2 \end{equation*}
    The area differential element transforms as: \(dxdy = \left| \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} \right| \, dudv = 2 \, dudv\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Step 2: Map the Region of Integration
    🔗
    The boundaries of the original region \(R\) in the \(xy\)-plane are given by the limits of the iterated integral:
    \begin{equation*} y = 0, \quad y = 4, \quad x = \frac{y}{2}, \quad x = \frac{y}{2} + 1 \end{equation*}
    We translate these boundary walls into the \(uv\)-plane using our substitutions:
    • The lower edge \(y = 0 \implies 2v = 0 \implies v = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The upper edge \(y = 4 \implies 2v = 4 \implies v = 2\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The left edge \(x = \frac{y}{2} \implies u + v = v \implies u = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The right edge \(x = \frac{y}{2} + 1 \implies u + v = v + 1 \implies u = 1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    This shows that the original skewed parallelogram region \(R\) maps to a simple rectangular domain \(S\) in the \(uv\)-plane: \(S = \{(u,v) : 0 \leq u \leq 1, \ 0 \leq v \leq 2\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Step 3: Setup and Evaluate the Transformed Integral
    🔗
    Substituting the new tracking variables, the integrand \(\frac{2x-y}{2} = u\text{,}\) the Jacobian factor, and our bounds into the theorem equation, we get:
    \begin{equation*} \iint_R \left(\frac{2x-y}{2}\right)\,dxdy = \int_{0}^{2} \int_{0}^{1} u \cdot \Big( 2 \, dudv \Big) \end{equation*}
    Since the constant bounds let us decouple the double integration into a simple single product setup:
    \begin{gather*} = 2 \left( \int_{0}^{2} \, dv \right) \left( \int_{0}^{1} u \, du \right)\\ = 2 \cdot \Big[ v \Big]_{0}^{2} \cdot \left[ \frac{1}{2}u^2 \right]_{0}^{1}\\ = 2 \cdot (2 - 0) \cdot \left( \frac{1}{2}(1)^2 - 0 \right)\\ = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 2. \end{gather*}
    The value of the double integral calculated via change of variables is exactly \(2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.82.

    Evaluate the double integral:
    \begin{equation*} \iint_R e^{(y-x)/(y+x)} \,dxdy, \end{equation*}
    where \(R\) is the region in the \(xy\)-plane bounded by the trapezoid with vertices \((0,1)\text{,}\) \((0,2)\text{,}\) \((2,0)\text{,}\) and \((1,0)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Step 1: Choose a Transformation and Find its Inverse
    🔗
    Looking at the structure of the integrand and the boundary behaviors of the trapezoid, we can simplify the problem by defining the new tracking variables:
    \begin{equation*} u = y - x \quad \text{and} \quad v = y + x. \end{equation*}
    We solve this system of linear equations for \(x\) and \(y\) in terms of \(u\) and \(v\) to find the inverse transformation equations:
    \begin{equation*} v + u = (y + x) + (y - x) = 2y \implies y = \frac{1}{2}(v + u) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} v - u = (y + x) - (y - x) = 2x \implies x = \frac{1}{2}(v - u) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Step 2: Calculate the Jacobian Matrix Determinant
    🔗
    Using Definition 4.77, we compute the partial derivative array:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial (u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} \amp \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} \amp \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} -\frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{1}{2} \amp \frac{1}{2} \end{vmatrix} = \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) - \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = -\frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{4} = -\frac{1}{2}. \end{equation*}
    Taking the absolute value, the area differential element transforms as: \(dxdy = \left| -\frac{1}{2} \right| \,dudv = \frac{1}{2}\,dudv\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Step 3: Map the Integration Regions
    🔗
    The trapezoidal region \(R\) in the \(xy\)-plane is bounded by four line segments: the \(y\)-axis (\(x=0\)), the \(x\)-axis (\(y=0\)), and the slanted parallel lines connecting the intercepts, which are \(x+y=1\) and \(x+y=2\text{.}\) Substituting our definitions for \(u\) and \(v\text{,}\) we find their values along these boundaries:
    • Along the boundary line \(x+y=1 \implies v = 1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Along the boundary line \(x+y=2 \implies v = 2\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Along the \(y\)-axis line \(x=0 \implies \frac{1}{2}(v-u) = 0 \implies u = v\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Along the \(x\)-axis line \(y=0 \implies \frac{1}{2}(v+u) = 0 \implies u = -v\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    This transforms the skewed trapezoid \(R\) into a simpler trapezoidal region \(S\) in the \(uv\)-plane bounded by the horizontal lines \(v=1, v=2\) and the rays \(u=-v, u=v\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Step 4: Set Up and Evaluate the Transformed Integral
    🔗
    We rewrite the iterated double integral using our new boundaries, substituting the integrand \(\frac{y-x}{y+x} = \frac{u}{v}\) and the area differential element \(dxdy = \frac{1}{2}\,dudv\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \iint_R e^{\frac{y-x}{y+x}} \,dxdy = \int_{1}^{2} \int_{-v}^{v} e^{u/v} \cdot \left( \frac{1}{2} \, dudv \right) \end{equation*}
    First, we integrate the inner expression with respect to \(u\text{,}\) treating \(v\) as a constant factor:
    \begin{align*} \int_{-v}^{v} \frac{1}{2} e^{u/v} \, du \amp = \left[ \frac{1}{2} \cdot v e^{u/v} \right]_{u=-v}^{u=v}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{2}v \left( e^{v/v} - e^{-v/v} \right) = \frac{1}{2}v \left( e^1 - e^{-1} \right). \end{align*}
    Next, we substitute this result into the outer structural layer and integrate with respect to \(v\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \int_{1}^{2} \frac{1}{2}v (e - e^{-1}) \, dv \amp = \frac{1}{2}(e - e^{-1}) \int_{1}^{2} v \, dv\\ \amp = \frac{1}{2}(e - e^{-1}) \left[ \frac{1}{2}v^2 \right]_{1}^{2}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{4}(e - e^{-1}) \Big( (2)^2 - (1)^2 \Big)\\ \amp = \frac{1}{4}(e - e^{-1})(4 - 1) = \frac{3}{4}(e - e^{-1}). \end{align*}
    The exact value of the double integral over the trapezoidal region is \(\frac{3}{4}(e - e^{-1})\text{.}\)
