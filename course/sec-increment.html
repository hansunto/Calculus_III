    Section 3.4 Increments and Differentials

    In this section, we will explore the concepts of increments and differentials, which are fundamental for analyzing how multi-variable functions respond to small changes in their independent inputs. We will begin by defining the exact increment of a function and establishing the rigorous criteria for differentiability using non-unique error functions.
    🔗
    If \(f\) is a function of two variables \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) then \(\Delta x\) and \(\Delta y\) denote the increments of \(x\) and \(y\). Suppose \(x\) changes from \(a\) to \(a+\Delta x\) and \(y\) changes from \(b\) to \(b+\Delta y\text{.}\) Then the corresponding increment of \(z=f(x,y)\) is:
    \begin{equation*} \Delta z=f(a+\Delta x, b+\Delta y)-f(a,b) \end{equation*}
    The increment of \(z\text{,}\) denoted \(\Delta z\text{,}\) represents the change in the values of \(f\) when \((x,y)\) changes from \((a,b)\) to \((a+\Delta x, b +\Delta y)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 3.41.

    Let \(z=f(x,y)=3x^2-xy\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Find an algebraic expression for \(\Delta z\) in terms of \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) \(\Delta x\text{,}\) and \(\Delta y\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Calculate the exact numerical value of \(\Delta z\) if \((x,y)\) changes from \((1,2)\) to \((1.01, 1.98)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. Using the definition of a multivariate function increment, we set up and expand the expression for \(\Delta z\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} \Delta z \amp = f(x+\Delta x, y+\Delta y) - f(x,y)\\ \amp = \left[3(x+\Delta x)^2 - (x+\Delta x)(y+\Delta y)\right] - (3x^2 - xy)\\ \amp = \left[3(x^2 + 2x\Delta x + (\Delta x)^2) - (xy + x\Delta y + y\Delta x + \Delta x\Delta y)\right] - 3x^2 + xy\\ \amp = 3x^2 + 6x\Delta x + 3(\Delta x)^2 - xy - x\Delta y - y\Delta x - \Delta x\Delta y - 3x^2 + xy \end{align*}
      Canceling the matching \(3x^2\) and \(xy\) terms, we obtain the simplified general increment:
      \begin{equation*} \Delta z = 6x\Delta x + 3(\Delta x)^2 - y\Delta x - x\Delta y - \Delta x\Delta y \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. The independent variables change from the base point \((x,y) = (1,2)\) to the final point \((1.01, 1.98)\text{.}\) We compute the respective component increments:
      \begin{equation*} \Delta x = 1.01 - 1 = 0.01 \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \Delta y = 1.98 - 2 = -0.02 \end{equation*}
      Substituting \(x=1\text{,}\) \(y=2\text{,}\) \(\Delta x = 0.01\text{,}\) and \(\Delta y = -0.02\) directly into our algebraic result from Part 1 yields:
      \begin{align*} \Delta z \amp = 6(1)(0.01) + 3(0.01)^2 - 2(0.01) - 1(-0.02) - (0.01)(-0.02)\\ \amp = 0.06 + 3(0.0001) - 0.02 + 0.02 - (-0.0002)\\ \amp = 0.06 + 0.0003 + 0.0002\\ \amp = 0.0605 \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.42. Differentiability for Functions of Two Variables.

    If \(z=f(x,y)\text{,}\) then \(f\) is differentiable at \((a,b)\) if \(\Delta z\) can be expressed in the form:
    \begin{equation*} \Delta z = f_x(a,b)\Delta x + f_y(a,b)\Delta y + \epsilon_1\Delta x + \epsilon_2\Delta y \end{equation*}
    where \(\epsilon_1 \to 0\) and \(\epsilon_2 \to 0\) as \((\Delta x,\Delta y) \to (0,0)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.43. Sufficient Condition for Differentiability.

    If the partial derivatives \(f_x\) and \(f_y\) exist near \((a,b)\) and are continuous at \((a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f\) is differentiable at \((a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.44.

    If \(z=f(x,y)=3x^2-xy\text{,}\) find an expression for \(\Delta z\) to identify the functions \(\epsilon_1\) and \(\epsilon_2\) using the base point \((a,b)=(x,y)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, we find the first-order partial derivatives of \(f(x,y) = 3x^2-xy\) to establish our baseline linear terms:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y) = 6x - y \quad \text{and} \quad f_y(x,y) = -x \end{equation*}
    🔗
    From our previous algebraic expansion of the increment for this exact function, we know that:
    \begin{equation*} \Delta z = 6x\Delta x + 3(\Delta x)^2 - y\Delta x - x\Delta y - \Delta x\Delta y \end{equation*}
    We rearrange and group these terms to isolate the linear differential components \(f_x(x,y)\Delta x\) and \(f_y(x,y)\Delta y\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \Delta z \amp = (6x\Delta x - y\Delta x) + (-x\Delta y) + 3(\Delta x)^2 - \Delta x\Delta y\\ \amp = (6x - y)\Delta x + (-x)\Delta y + 3(\Delta x)^2 - \Delta x\Delta y \end{align*}
    🔗
    To match the definition’s template form of \(\epsilon_1\Delta x + \epsilon_2\Delta y\text{,}\) we factor a \(\Delta x\) out of the first remaining term and a \(\Delta y\) out of the second remaining term:
    \begin{equation*} \Delta z = (6x - y)\Delta x + (-x)\Delta y + (3\Delta x)\Delta x + (-\Delta x)\Delta y \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Comparing this directly to the definition form, we can identify:
    \begin{equation*} \epsilon_1 = 3\Delta x \quad \text{and} \quad \epsilon_2 = -\Delta x \end{equation*}
    As \((\Delta x, \Delta y) \to (0,0)\text{,}\) it is clear that both \(\epsilon_1 \to 0\) and \(\epsilon_2 \to 0\text{.}\) This confirms explicitly that the polynomial function \(f(x,y) = 3x^2-xy\) is differentiable everywhere.
    🔗
    Note: Because the leftover terms can be grouped in alternative arrangements (for example, factoring \(\Delta x\) out of the entire remaining expression), the error functions \(\epsilon_1\) and \(\epsilon_2\) are not unique.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.45. Differentials for Functions of Two Variables.

    If \(z=f(x,y)\) is a differentiable function, the differentials \(dx\) and \(dy\) are independent variables defined as \(dx=\Delta x\) and \(dy=\Delta y\text{.}\) The total differential of \(z\) (or the dependent differential \(dz\)) is defined by:
    \begin{equation*} dz = f_x(x,y)\,dx + f_y(x,y)\,dy = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\,dx + \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\,dy \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Using this framework, the exact function increment can be written as:
    \begin{equation*} \Delta z = f_x(a,b)\Delta x + f_y(a,b)\Delta y + \epsilon_1\Delta x + \epsilon_2\Delta y = dz + \epsilon_1\Delta x + \epsilon_2\Delta y \end{equation*}
    On the other hand, the discrepancy between the exact increment and the total differential approximation is given by:
    \begin{equation*} \Delta z - dz = \epsilon_1\Delta x + \epsilon_2\Delta y \end{equation*}
    where \(\epsilon_1 \to 0\) and \(\epsilon_2 \to 0\) as \((\Delta x,\Delta y) \to (0,0)\text{.}\) Therefore, for small changes in \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) we can approximate the true change using the differential:
    \begin{equation*} \Delta z \approx dz \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.46.

    If \(z=f(x,y)=3x^2-xy\text{,}\) find the total differential \(dz\text{.}\) Then, use it to approximate the change in \(z\) if \((x,y)\) changes from \((1,2)\) to \((1.01, 1.98)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, we find the first-order partial derivatives of the function \(f(x,y) = 3x^2-xy\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y) = 6x - y \quad \text{and} \quad f_y(x,y) = -x \end{equation*}
    Substituting these expressions into the definition of the total differential yields:
    \begin{equation*} dz = (6x - y)\,dx + (-x)\,dy \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Next, we use the given points to determine the baseline values and independent changes:
    \begin{equation*} x = 1, \quad y = 2 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} dx = \Delta x = 1.01 - 1 = 0.01 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} dy = \Delta y = 1.98 - 2 = -0.02 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Substituting these specific numerical parameters directly into our total differential expression approximates the change:
    \begin{align*} dz \amp = (6(1) - 2)(0.01) + (-1)(-0.02)\\ \amp = (4)(0.01) + 0.02\\ \amp = 0.04 + 0.02\\ \amp = 0.06 \end{align*}
    Thus, the approximate change in \(z\) computed via differentials is \(dz = 0.06\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To find the error involved in using this approximation, we compare it against the exact change (\(\Delta z = 0.0605\)) calculated in our previous exercise:
    \begin{equation*} \text{Error} = |\Delta z - dz| = |0.0605 - 0.06| = 0.0005 \end{equation*}
    This demonstrates that for small structural changes near the point \((1,2)\text{,}\) the total differential provides a highly accurate approximation.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.47.

    The radius and altitude of a right circular cylinder are measured as \(3\text{ in}\) and \(8\text{ in}\) respectively, with a maximum possible error in each measurement of \(\pm 0.05\text{ inch}\text{.}\) Use differentials to approximate the maximum error in the calculated volume of the cylinder.
    🔗
    Solution.
    The volume \(V\) of a right circular cylinder is a function of two variables—its radius \(r\) and its height or altitude \(h\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} V(r,h) = \pi r^2 h \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The total differential \(dV\) represents the approximate propagated error in the volume and is found by calculating the first-order partial derivatives with respect to \(r\) and \(h\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} dV \amp = \frac{\partial V}{\partial r}\,dr + \frac{\partial V}{\partial h}\,dh\\ \amp = (2\pi r h)\,dr + (\pi r^2)\,dh \end{align*}
    🔗
    From the problem statements, we isolate our baseline parameters and maximum independent measurement fluctuations:
    \begin{equation*} r = 3, \quad h = 8, \quad |dr| \leq 0.05, \quad |dh| \leq 0.05 \end{equation*}
    To approximate the maximum potential error, we look at the worst-case scenario where the errors accumulate constructively, taking the maximum absolute value bounds for the differentials (\(dr = 0.05\) and \(dh = 0.05\)):
    \begin{align*} dV \amp = 2\pi(3)(8)(0.05) + \pi(3)^2(0.05)\\ \amp = 48\pi(0.05) + 9\pi(0.05)\\ \amp = 2.4\pi + 0.45\pi\\ \amp = 2.85\pi \approx 8.9535\text{ in}^3 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Thus, using differentials, the estimated maximum error in the calculated volume is approximately \(\pm 2.85\pi\text{ cubic inches}\) (or roughly \(\pm 8.95\text{ in}^3\)).
