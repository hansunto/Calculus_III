    Section 4.5 Cylindrical Coordinates

    Cylindrical coordinates are a three-dimensional coordinate system that extends polar coordinates by adding a third coordinate for height. They are particularly useful for problems with cylindrical symmetry.
    🔗
    In the cylindrical coordinate system, a point \(P\) in three-dimensional space is represented by the ordered triple \((r,\theta,z)\text{,}\) where \(r\) and \(\theta\) are polar coordinates of the projection of \(P\) onto the \(xy\)-plane and \(z\) is the directed distance from the \(xy\)-plane to \(P\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 4.60. Rectangular and Cylindrical Relationships.

    The rectangular coordinates \((x,y,z)\) and the cylindrical coordinates \((r,\theta,z)\) of a point \(P\) are related as follows:
    \begin{equation*} x=r\cos \theta, \quad y=r\sin \theta, \quad \tan \theta =\frac{y}{x}, \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x^2+y^2=r^2, \quad z=z. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.61.

    Find an equation in cylindrical coordinates for the ellipsoid
    \begin{equation*} 4x^2+4y^2+z^2=1. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    We can find the cylindrical equation by factoring out the common coefficients from the first two terms to isolate the standard polar identity:
    \begin{equation*} 4(x^2 + y^2) + z^2 = 1 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Using the relationship from Theorem 4.60, we substitute \(x^2 + y^2 = r^2\) into our equation:
    \begin{equation*} 4r^2 + z^2 = 1 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Thus, the equation of the ellipsoid in cylindrical coordinates is \(4r^2 + z^2 = 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 4.62. Evaluation Theorem in Cylindrical Coordinates.

    \begin{equation*} \iiint_Q f(x,y,z)\,dV = \int^{\beta}_{\alpha} \int^{h_2(\theta)}_{h_1(\theta)} \int^{u_2(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)}_{u_1(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)} f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, z)r\,dzdrd\theta. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.63.

    Evaluate the iterated triple integral in cylindrical coordinates:
    \begin{equation*} \int^{2\pi}_{0} \int^{1}_{0} \int^{1/2}_{-1/2} (r^2\sin^2\theta+z^2)r \, dzdrd\theta. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    Because all six integration limits are constant bounds, the domain of integration represents a solid cylinder of radius \(1\) and height \(1\) centered on the \(z\)-axis. We distribute the extra radial factor \(r\) into the integrand and evaluate from the inside out:
    \begin{equation*} = \int^{2\pi}_{0} \int^{1}_{0} \int^{1/2}_{-1/2} (r^3\sin^2\theta + rz^2) \, dzdrd\theta. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Step 1: Evaluate the Inner Integral with respect to \(z\)
    🔗
    Treating \(r\) and \(\theta\) as constant parameters:
    \begin{align*} \int^{1/2}_{-1/2} (r^3\sin^2\theta + rz^2) \, dz \amp = \left[ r^3\sin^2\theta \cdot z + \frac{1}{3}rz^3 \right]_{z=-1/2}^{z=1/2}\\ \amp = \left( r^3\sin^2\theta\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) + \frac{1}{3}r\left(\frac{1}{8}\right) \right) - \left( r^3\sin^2\theta\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right) + \frac{1}{3}r\left(-\frac{1}{8}\right) \right)\\ \amp = \frac{1}{2}r^3\sin^2\theta + \frac{1}{24}r + \frac{1}{2}r^3\sin^2\theta + \frac{1}{24}r\\ \amp = r^3\sin^2\theta + \frac{1}{12}r. \end{align*}
    🔗
    Step 2: Evaluate the Middle Integral with respect to \(r\)
    🔗
    We substitute our expression from Step 1 and integrate with respect to \(r\text{,}\) keeping \(\theta\) constant:
    \begin{align*} \int_{0}^{1} \left( r^3\sin^2\theta + \frac{1}{12}r \right) \, dr \amp = \left[ \frac{1}{4}r^4\sin^2\theta + \frac{1}{24}r^2 \right]_{0}^{1}\\ \amp = \left( \frac{1}{4}(1)^4\sin^2\theta + \frac{1}{24}(1)^2 \right) - 0\\ \amp = \frac{1}{4}\sin^2\theta + \frac{1}{24}. \end{align*}
    🔗
    Step 3: Evaluate the Outer Integral with respect to \(\theta\)
    🔗
    Finally, we evaluate across the angular domain. We split the terms and use the trigonometric power-reduction identity \(\sin^2\theta = \frac{1-\cos(2\theta)}{2}\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \int_{0}^{2\pi} \left( \frac{1}{4}\sin^2\theta + \frac{1}{24} \right) \, d\theta \amp = \frac{1}{4} \int_{0}^{2\pi} \left( \frac{1-\cos(2\theta)}{2} \right) \, d\theta + \frac{1}{24} \int_{0}^{2\pi} \, d\theta\\ \amp = \frac{1}{8} \Big[ \theta - \frac{1}{2}\sin(2\theta) \Big]_{0}^{2\pi} + \frac{1}{24} \Big[ \theta \Big]_{0}^{2\pi}\\ \amp = \frac{1}{8} \Big( (2\pi - 0) - 0 \Big) + \frac{1}{24}(2\pi - 0)\\ \amp = \frac{2\pi}{8} + \frac{2\pi}{24} = \frac{\pi}{4} + \frac{\pi}{12} = \frac{3\pi + \pi}{12} = \frac{4\pi}{12} = \frac{\pi}{3}. \end{align*}
    The exact value of the cylindrical triple integral is \(\frac{\pi}{3}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.64.

    Find the volume of the solid that lies within the cylinder \(x^2+y^2=1\text{,}\) below the plane \(z=4\text{,}\) and above the paraboloid \(z=1-x^2-y^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Step 1: Identify the Boundaries and Region in Cylindrical Coordinates
    🔗
    The lateral boundary is the cylinder \(x^2+y^2=1\text{,}\) which simplifies directly to \(r = 1\) in polar form. This describes a full circular disk base region \(R\) in the \(xy\)-plane:
    \begin{equation*} 0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi \quad \text{and} \quad 0 \leq r \leq 1. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The solid is bounded above by the flat plane ceiling \(z = 4\) and capped below by the paraboloid floor surface. Converting the floor equation to cylindrical form using \(x^2+y^2=r^2\) yields:
    \begin{equation*} z = 1 - (x^2 + y^2) \implies z = 1 - r^2. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, for any point \((r, \theta)\) in the base circle, the vertical variable \(z\) spans:
    \begin{equation*} 1 - r^2 \leq z \leq 4. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Step 2: Set Up and Evaluate the Volume Integral
    🔗
    Using the volume double integral definition with the cylindrical volume element (\(dV = r\,dzdrd\theta\)), we establish the iterated integral:
    \begin{equation*} V = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \int_{0}^{1} \int_{1-r^2}^{4} r \, dz \, dr \, d\theta \end{equation*}
    Evaluating the inner integral with respect to \(z\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \int_{1-r^2}^{4} r \, dz = r \Big[ z \Big]_{1-r^2}^{4} = r \Big( 4 - (1 - r^2) \Big) = r(3 + r^2) = 3r + r^3. \end{equation*}
    Next, substituting into the middle layer and integrating with respect to \(r\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \int_{0}^{1} (3r + r^3) \, dr = \left[ \frac{3}{2}r^2 + \frac{1}{4}r^4 \right]_{0}^{1} = \frac{3}{2} + \frac{1}{4} - 0 = \frac{6+1}{4} = \frac{7}{4}. \end{equation*}
    Finally, evaluating the outer angular layer:
    \begin{equation*} V = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \frac{7}{4} \, d\theta = \frac{7}{4} \Big[ \theta \Big]_{0}^{2\pi} = \frac{7}{4}(2\pi) = \frac{7\pi}{2}. \end{equation*}
    The total volume of the solid is exactly \(\frac{7\pi}{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 4.66.

    Evaluate the iterated triple integral by converting it to cylindrical coordinates:
    \begin{equation*} \int^2_{-2}\int^{\sqrt{4-x^2}}_{-\sqrt{4-x^2}}\int^{2}_{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}(x^2+y^2)\,dzdydx. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    Step 1: Translate the Boundaries to Cylindrical Coordinates
    🔗
    The outer Cartesian boundaries describe the base region shadow footprint \(R\) in the \(xy\)-plane:
    \begin{equation*} -2 \leq x \leq 2 \quad \text{and} \quad -\sqrt{4-x^2} \leq y \leq \sqrt{4-x^2}. \end{equation*}
    This matches a complete circular disk of radius \(r = 2\) centered at the origin, which translates into constant polar tracking boundaries:
    \begin{equation*} 0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi \quad \text{and} \quad 0 \leq r \leq 2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The inner vertical limits bound \(z\) below by the cone surface \(z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}\) and above by the flat plane ceiling \(z = 2\text{.}\) Substituting \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2} = r\) gives:
    \begin{equation*} r \leq z \leq 2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Step 2: Convert and Evaluate the Iterated Integral
    🔗
    We substitute the inner integrand term \(x^2 + y^2 = r^2\) and change the differential string to cylindrical form (\(dzdydx = r\,dzdrd\theta\)):
    \begin{equation*} \iiint_Q (x^2+y^2)\,dV = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \int_{0}^{2} \int_{r}^{2} (r^2) \cdot r \, dz \, dr \, d\theta = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \int_{0}^{2} \int_{r}^{2} r^3 \, dz \, dr \, d\theta \end{equation*}
    Evaluating the innermost variable layer with respect to \(z\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \int_{r}^{2} r^3 \, dz = r^3 \Big[ z \Big]_{r}^{2} = r^3(2 - r) = 2r^3 - r^4. \end{equation*}
    Substituting this polynomial result into the middle layer and integrating with respect to \(r\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \int_{0}^{2} (2r^3 - r^4) \, dr = \left[ \frac{1}{2}r^4 - \frac{1}{5}r^5 \right]_{0}^{2} = \left( \frac{1}{2}(16) - \frac{1}{5}(32) \right) - 0 = 8 - \frac{32}{5} = \frac{40 - 32}{5} = \frac{8}{5}. \end{equation*}
    Finally, evaluating the outer decoupled angular integral layer:
    \begin{equation*} \int_{0}^{2\pi} \frac{8}{5} \, d\theta = \frac{8}{5} \Big[ \theta \Big]_{0}^{2\pi} = \frac{8}{5}(2\pi) = \frac{16\pi}{5}. \end{equation*}
    The exact evaluation of the transformed cylindrical integral is \(\frac{16\pi}{5}\text{.}\)

    1.

    Find the volume \(V\) of the solid region \(Q\) shown in the figure below. The region is bounded above by a paraboloid ceiling and below by a shallow quartic bowl, meeting at a shared intersection ring on the \(xy\)-plane.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Step 1: Extract Equations from the Figure and Determine the Setup
    🔗
    Reading the label vectors from the figure, the solid region \(Q\) is bounded:
    • Above by the paraboloid ceiling: \(z = 4 - 4(x^2 + y^2)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Below by the quartic bowl floor: \(z = (x^2 + y^2)^2 - 1\)
      🔗
      🔗
    We convert these boundaries into cylindrical coordinates using the polar identity \(x^2 + y^2 = r^2\text{,}\) which yields \(z = 4 - 4r^2\) for the top and \(z = r^4 - 1\) for the bottom.
    🔗
    Step 2: Find the Radial Limits of Integration
    🔗
    The figure illustrates that the surfaces meet symmetrically at a boundary curve in the \(xy\)-plane (\(z=0\)). To find this intersection ring radius, we set the floor equation equal to the ceiling equation:
    \begin{equation*} r^4 - 1 = 4 - 4r^2 \implies r^4 + 4r^2 - 5 = 0 \end{equation*}
    Factoring this polynomial expression as a quadratic in terms of \(r^2\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} (r^2 + 5)(r^2 - 1) = 0 \end{equation*}
    Since \(r^2\) must be a non-negative real value, we find \(r^2 = 1 \implies r = 1\text{.}\) This yields a complete, full base disk footprint. The cylindrical limits of integration are:
    \begin{equation*} 0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi, \quad 0 \leq r \leq 1, \quad r^4 - 1 \leq z \leq 4 - 4r^2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Step 3: Evaluate the Triple Integral
    🔗
    Using the cylindrical differential volume tracking element (\(dV = r\,dzdrd\theta\)), we set up and compute the volume:
    \begin{equation*} V = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \int_{0}^{1} \int_{r^4 - 1}^{4 - 4r^2} r \, dz \, dr \, d\theta \end{equation*}
    Integrating the inner layer with respect to \(z\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \int_{r^4 - 1}^{4 - 4r^2} r \, dz = r \Big[ z \Big]_{r^4 - 1}^{4 - 4r^2} = r \Big( (4 - 4r^2) - (r^4 - 1) \Big) = 5r - 4r^3 - r^5. \end{equation*}
    We split the remaining decoupled double integral into a simple product of two independent single integrals:
    \begin{align*} V \amp = \left( \int_{0}^{2\pi} \, d\theta \right) \left( \int_{0}^{1} (5r - 4r^3 - r^5) \, dr \right)\\ \amp = \Big[ \theta \Big]_{0}^{2\pi} \cdot \left[ \frac{5}{2}r^2 - r^4 - \frac{1}{6}r^6 \right]_{0}^{1}\\ \amp = (2\pi - 0) \cdot \left( \frac{5}{2}(1)^2 - (1)^4 - \frac{1}{6}(1)^6 - 0 \right)\\ \amp = 2\pi \cdot \left( \frac{15}{6} - \frac{6}{6} - \frac{1}{6} \right) = 2\pi \cdot \frac{8}{6} = \frac{8\pi}{3}. \end{align*}
    The total volume of the solid shown in the figure is exactly \(\frac{8\pi}{3}\text{.}\)

    2.

    Find the volume \(V\) of the solid wedge bounded below by the \(xy\)-plane (\(z=0\)), laterally by the circular cylinder \(r = 3\cos\theta\text{,}\) and above by the slanted plane \(z = -y\) inside the octant where \(x \gt 0\text{,}\) \(y \lt 0\text{,}\) and \(z \gt 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Step 1: Verify the Octant Constraints and Integration Bounds
    🔗
    The geometric restrictions are defined by the region \(x \gt 0\text{,}\) \(y \lt 0\text{,}\) and \(z \gt 0\text{.}\) This confines the base of the solid to the fourth quadrant of the \(xy\)-plane.
    🔗
    The lateral boundary is the off-center cylinder \(r = 3\cos\theta\text{.}\) In polar terms, this circle is fully traced on the horizontal interval \(-\frac{\pi}{2} \leq \theta \leq \frac{\pi}{2}\text{.}\) Restricting this base circle to the fourth quadrant (\(y \lt 0\)) isolates our angular integration interval to:
    \begin{equation*} -\frac{\pi}{2} \leq \theta \leq 0. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Inside this angle range, the radius \(r\) starts at the origin and sweeps outward to intersect the cylinder wall \(r = 3\cos\theta\text{.}\) The ceiling plane equation \(z = -y\) translates directly to cylindrical form as \(z = -r\sin\theta\text{.}\) Because \(\theta\) is negative in the fourth quadrant, \(\sin\theta \lt 0\text{,}\) which correctly ensures that the height \(z = -r\sin\theta\) remains strictly positive (\(z \gt 0\)). The final bounds are:
    \begin{equation*} -\frac{\pi}{2} \leq \theta \leq 0, \quad 0 \leq r \leq 3\cos\theta, \quad 0 \leq z \leq -r\sin\theta. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Step 2: Evaluate the Cylindrical Volume Integral
    🔗
    Using the cylindrical volume element configuration (\(dV = r\,dzdrd\theta\)), we set up the iterated double integral:
    \begin{equation*} V = \int_{-\pi/2}^{0} \int_{0}^{3\cos\theta} \int_{0}^{-r\sin\theta} r \, dz \, dr \, d\theta \end{equation*}
    Evaluating the innermost variable layer with respect to \(z\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \int_{0}^{-r\sin\theta} r \, dz = r \Big[ z \Big]_{0}^{-r\sin\theta} = -r^2\sin\theta. \end{equation*}
    Substituting this into the middle layer and integrating with respect to \(r\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \int_{0}^{3\cos\theta} (-r^2\sin\theta) \, dr \amp = -\sin\theta \left[ \frac{1}{3}r^3 \right]_{0}^{3\cos\theta} = -\frac{1}{3}\sin\theta \Big( 27\cos^3\theta \Big)\\ \amp = -9\cos^3\theta\sin\theta. \end{align*}
    Finally, we evaluate the outer angular layer with respect to \(\theta\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} V = \int_{-\pi/2}^{0} -9\cos^3\theta\sin\theta \, d\theta \end{equation*}
    We solve this using a basic \(u\)-substitution. Let:
    \begin{equation*} u = \cos\theta \implies du = -\sin\theta \, d\theta \implies -du = \sin\theta \, d\theta. \end{equation*}
    Updating our definite boundaries: when \(\theta = -\frac{\pi}{2} \implies u = 0\text{,}\) and when \(\theta = 0 \implies u = 1\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} V \amp = \int_{0}^{1} 9u^3 \, du = \left[ \frac{9}{4}u^4 \right]_{0}^{1}\\ \amp = \frac{9}{4}(1)^4 - 0 = \frac{9}{4}. \end{align*}
    The total volume of the solid wedge within the specified octant is exactly \(\frac{9}{4}\) (or \(2.25\)).

    3.

    Find the volume of the region enclosed by the cylinder \(x^2+y^2=4\) and the planes \(z=0\) and \(y+z=4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Step 1: Translate Boundaries to Cylindrical Coordinates
    🔗
    The lateral boundary is a solid cylinder of radius \(2\) centered at the origin, which describes a complete circular disk base region \(R\) in the \(xy\)-plane:
    \begin{equation*} 0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi \quad \text{and} \quad 0 \leq r \leq 2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The solid is bounded below by the floor plane \(z = 0\) and capped above by the slanted plane ceiling. Isolating \(z\) from the plane equation and converting to cylindrical coordinates using the standard substitution rule \(y = r\sin\theta\) gives:
    \begin{equation*} y + z = 4 \implies z = 4 - y \implies z = 4 - r\sin\theta. \end{equation*}
    Therefore, for any point \((r, \theta)\) in the base circle, the vertical variable \(z\) spans:
    \begin{equation*} 0 \leq z \leq 4 - r\sin\theta. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Step 2: Set Up and Evaluate the Volume Integral
    🔗
    Using the volume triple integral definition with the cylindrical volume element (\(dV = r\,dzdrd\theta\)), we establish the iterated integral:
    \begin{equation*} V = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \int_{0}^{2} \int_{0}^{4-r\sin\theta} r \, dz \, dr \, d\theta \end{equation*}
    Evaluating the innermost layer with respect to \(z\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \int_{0}^{4-r\sin\theta} r \, dz = r \Big[ z \Big]_{0}^{4-r\sin\theta} = r(4 - r\sin\theta) = 4r - r^2\sin\theta. \end{equation*}
    Substituting this result into the middle layer and integrating with respect to \(r\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} \int_{0}^{2} (4r - r^2\sin\theta) \, dr \amp = \left[ 2r^2 - \frac{1}{3}r^3\sin\theta \right]_{0}^{2}\\ \amp = \left( 2(2)^2 - \frac{1}{3}(2)^3\sin\theta \right) - 0\\ \amp = 8 - \frac{8}{3}\sin\theta. \end{align*}
    Finally, computing across the complete angular revolution outer loop:
    \begin{align*} V \amp = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \left( 8 - \frac{8}{3}\sin\theta \right) \, d\theta\\ \amp = \left[ 8\theta + \frac{8}{3}\cos\theta \right]_{0}^{2\pi}\\ \amp = \left( 8(2\pi) + \frac{8}{3}\cos(2\pi) \right) - \left( 0 + \frac{8}{3}\cos(0) \right)\\ \amp = \left( 16\pi + \frac{8}{3} \right) - \frac{8}{3} = 16\pi. \end{align*}
    The total volume of the region enclosed by the cylinder and the planes is exactly \(16\pi\text{.}\)
