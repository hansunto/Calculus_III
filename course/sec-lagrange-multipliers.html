    Section 3.9 Lagrange Multipliers

    In this section, we will explore the method of Lagrange multipliers, which is a powerful technique for finding the local maxima and minima of a function subject to equality constraints. This method is particularly useful in optimization problems where we want to maximize or minimize a function while satisfying certain conditions.
    🔗
    Let \(f(x,y)\) be the temperature at the point \(P(x,y)\) on a flat metal plate, and let \(C\) be a curve that has the equation \(g(x,y)=0\text{.}\) Suppose we wish to find the points on \(C\) at which the temperature attains its largest or smallest value. This amounts to finding the extrema of \(f(x,y)\) subject to the constraint (side condition) \(g(x,y)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 3.103. Lagrange Multipliers Theorem.

    Suppose \(f\) and \(g\) are functions of two variables that have continuous first-order partial derivatives, and that \(\nabla g \neq \mathbf{0}\) throughout a region of the \(xy\)-plane. If \(f\) has an extremum \(f(x_0,y_0)\) subject to the constraint \(g(x,y)=0\text{,}\) then there is a real number \(\lambda\) such that:
    \begin{equation*} \nabla f(x_0,y_0) = \lambda \nabla g(x_0,y_0) \end{equation*}
    The number \(\lambda\) is called a Lagrange multiplier.
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 3.104. The Method of Lagrange Multipliers.

    To find the maximum and minimum values of \(f(x,y)\) subject to the constraint \(g(x,y)=0\) (assuming that these extreme values exist and \(\nabla g \neq \mathbf{0}\) on the curve \(g(x,y)=0\)):
    🔗
    1. Find all values of \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(\lambda\) such that the following system of equations is satisfied:
      \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} f_x(x,y) \amp = \lambda g_x(x,y) \\ f_y(x,y) \amp = \lambda g_y(x,y) \\ g(x,y) \amp = 0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Evaluate \(f\) at all the points \((x,y)\) that result from Step 1. The largest of these values is the absolute maximum value of \(f\text{;}\) the smallest is the absolute minimum value of \(f\) subject to the constraint.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.105.

    Find the extrema of \(f(x,y)=xy\) if \((x,y)\) is restricted to the ellipse \(4x^2+y^2=4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We define our constraint function as \(g(x,y) = 4x^2 + y^2 - 4 = 0\text{.}\) Next, we compute the first-order partial derivatives of both \(f\) and \(g\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} f_x = y, \quad f_y = x \quad \text{and} \quad g_x = 8x, \quad g_y = 2y \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Applying the Method of Lagrange Multipliers, we set up the corresponding system of equations:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} y \amp = 8\lambda x \quad \text{(Eq. 1)} \\ x \amp = 2\lambda y \quad \text{(Eq. 2)} \\ 4x^2 + y^2 \amp = 4 \quad \text{(Eq. 3)} \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If \(x = 0\text{,}\) then Eq. 1 implies \(y = 0\text{,}\) but the point \((0,0)\) does not satisfy Eq. 3. Thus, \(x \neq 0\) and \(y \neq 0\text{,}\) which means \(\lambda \neq 0\text{.}\) We isolate \(\lambda\) from Eq. 1 and Eq. 2 to equate them:
    \begin{equation*} \lambda = \frac{y}{8x} = \frac{x}{2y} \implies 2y^2 = 8x^2 \implies y^2 = 4x^2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Substituting \(y^2 = 4x^2\) directly into our constraint equation (Eq. 3) yields:
    \begin{equation*} 4x^2 + (4x^2) = 4 \implies 8x^2 = 4 \implies x^2 = \frac{1}{2} \implies x = \pm \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{equation*}
    Using \(y^2 = 4x^2\text{,}\) we find that \(y^2 = 4\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = 2 \implies y = \pm \sqrt{2}\text{.}\) This produces four candidate critical points:
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \sqrt{2}\right), \quad \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, -\sqrt{2}\right), \quad \left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \sqrt{2}\right), \quad \left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, -\sqrt{2}\right) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Finally, we evaluate the objective function \(f(x,y) = xy\) at these points:
    • \(\displaystyle f\left(\pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \pm\sqrt{2}\right) = \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right)(\sqrt{2}) = 1\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle f\left(\pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \mp\sqrt{2}\right) = \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right)(-\sqrt{2}) = -1\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Therefore, the maximum value of \(f\) subject to the constraint is \(1\text{,}\) and the minimum value is \(-1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.106. Lagrange Multipliers for Three Variables.

    Suppose \(f\) and \(g\) are functions of three variables that have continuous first-order partial derivatives, and that \(\nabla g \neq \mathbf{0}\) throughout a region of the \(xyz\)-plane. If \(f\) has an extremum \(f(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) subject to the constraint \(g(x,y,z)=0\text{,}\) then there is a real number \(\lambda\) such that:
    \begin{equation*} \nabla f(x_0,y_0,z_0) = \lambda \nabla g(x_0,y_0,z_0) \end{equation*}
    The number \(\lambda\) is called a Lagrange multiplier.
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 3.107. The Method of Lagrange Multipliers: Three Variables.

    To find the maximum and minimum values of \(f(x,y,z)\) subject to the constraint \(g(x,y,z)=0\) (assuming that these values exist and \(\nabla g \neq \mathbf{0}\) on the surface \(g(x,y,z)=0\)):
    🔗
    1. Find all values of \(x, y, z,\) and \(\lambda\) such that:
      \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} f_x(x,y,z) \amp = \lambda g_x(x,y,z) \\ f_y(x,y,z) \amp = \lambda g_y(x,y,z) \\ f_z(x,y,z) \amp = \lambda g_z(x,y,z) \\ g(x,y,z) \amp = 0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Evaluate \(f\) at all the points \((x,y,z)\) that result from Step 1. The largest of these values is the maximum value of \(f\text{;}\) the smallest is the minimum value of \(f\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.108.

    A rectangular box without a lid is to be made from \(12\text{ m}^2\) of cardboard. Find the maximum volume of such a box.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(z\) denote the length, width, and height of the box respectively. We want to maximize the volume function:
    \begin{equation*} f(x,y,z) = V = xyz \end{equation*}
    subject to the surface area constraint that accounts for an open top (one bottom face and four side faces totaling \(12\text{ m}^2\)):
    \begin{equation*} g(x,y,z) = xy + 2xz + 2yz - 12 = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We find the partial derivatives for our Lagrange configuration:
    \begin{equation*} f_x = yz, \quad f_y = xz, \quad f_z = xy \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} g_x = y + 2z, \quad g_y = x + 2z, \quad g_z = 2x + 2y \end{equation*}
    This produces the following system of equations:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} yz \amp = \lambda(y + 2z) \quad \text{(Eq. 1)} \\ xz \amp = \lambda(x + 2z) \quad \text{(Eq. 2)} \\ xy \amp = \lambda(2x + 2y) \quad \text{(Eq. 3)} \\ xy + 2xz + 2yz \amp = 12 \quad \text{(Eq. 4)} \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since the dimensions must be strictly positive (\(x,y,z > 0\)), \(\lambda \neq 0\text{.}\) We multiply Eq. 1 by \(x\text{,}\) Eq. 2 by \(y\text{,}\) and Eq. 3 by \(z\) to create matching left-hand expressions:
    \begin{equation*} xyz = \lambda(xy + 2xz) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} xyz = \lambda(xy + 2yz) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} xyz = \lambda(2xz + 2yz) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Equating the first two expressions gives:
    \begin{equation*} \lambda(xy + 2xz) = \lambda(xy + 2yz) \implies 2xz = 2yz \implies x = y \end{equation*}
    Equating the second and third expressions gives:
    \begin{equation*} \lambda(xy + 2yz) = \lambda(2xz + 2yz) \implies xy = 2xz \implies y = 2z \end{equation*}
    Thus, we find the core dimension ratios: \(x = y = 2z\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We substitute these variables in terms of \(z\) into our constraint equation (Eq. 4):
    \begin{align*} (2z)(2z) + 2(2z)z + 2(2z)z \amp = 12\\ 4z^2 + 4z^2 + 4z^2 \amp = 12\\ 12z^2 \amp = 12 \implies z^2 = 1 \implies z = 1\text{ m} \end{align*}
    Using our ratios, the remaining dimensions are \(x = 2\text{ m}\) and \(y = 2\text{ m}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Finally, we evaluate the maximum volume:
    \begin{equation*} V = xyz = (2)(2)(1) = 4\text{ m}^3 \end{equation*}
    Thus, the maximum volume of the box is exactly \(4\text{ cubic meters}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.109.

    Find the extreme values of the function \(f(x,y)=x^2+2y^2\) on the circle \(x^2+y^2=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We define our constraint function as \(g(x,y) = x^2 + y^2 - 1 = 0\text{.}\) Next, we compute the first-order partial derivatives of both functions:
    \begin{equation*} f_x = 2x, \quad f_y = 4y \quad \text{and} \quad g_x = 2x, \quad g_y = 2y \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Applying the Method of Lagrange Multipliers, we set up the system of equations:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} 2x \amp = 2\lambda x \quad \text{(Eq. 1)} \\ 4y \amp = 2\lambda y \quad \text{(Eq. 2)} \\ x^2 + y^2 \amp = 1 \quad \text{(Eq. 3)} \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    From Eq. 1, we can group the terms to solve for case conditions:
    \begin{equation*} 2x(1 - \lambda) = 0 \implies x = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad \lambda = 1 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Case 1: \(x = 0\). Substituting this into Eq. 3 yields:
    \begin{equation*} 0^2 + y^2 = 1 \implies y = \pm 1 \end{equation*}
    This produces two candidate points: \((0, 1)\) and \((0, -1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Case 2: \(\lambda = 1\). Substituting this into Eq. 2 yields:
    \begin{equation*} 4y = 2(1)y \implies 2y = 0 \implies y = 0 \end{equation*}
    Substituting \(y = 0\) back into Eq. 3 yields:
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + 0^2 = 1 \implies x = \pm 1 \end{equation*}
    This produces two more candidate points: \((1, 0)\) and \((-1, 0)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Finally, we evaluate the objective function \(f(x,y) = x^2 + 2y^2\) at all four candidate points:
    • \(\displaystyle f(0, \pm 1) = 0^2 + 2(\pm 1)^2 = 2\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle f(\pm 1, 0) = (\pm 1)^2 + 2(0)^2 = 1\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Therefore, the maximum value of \(f\) on the circle is \(2\) (occurring at \((0, \pm 1)\)), and the minimum value is \(1\) (occurring at \((\pm 1, 0)\)).
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.110.

    Find the points on the sphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=4\) that are closest to and farthest from the point \((3,1,-1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The distance from any point \((x,y,z)\) to \((3,1,-1)\) is minimized or maximized when the squared distance is minimized or maximized. We define our objective function as:
    \begin{equation*} f(x,y,z) = (x-3)^2 + (y-1)^2 + (z+1)^2 \end{equation*}
    subject to the constraint describing the surface of the sphere:
    \begin{equation*} g(x,y,z) = x^2 + y^2 + z^2 - 4 = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We compute the partial derivatives for both functions:
    \begin{equation*} f_x = 2(x-3), \quad f_y = 2(y-1), \quad f_z = 2(z+1) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} g_x = 2x, \quad g_y = 2y, \quad g_z = 2z \end{equation*}
    Setting up our Lagrange multiplier system:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} 2(x-3) \amp = 2\lambda x \implies x - 3 = \lambda x \quad \text{(Eq. 1)} \\ 2(y-1) \amp = 2\lambda y \implies y - 1 = \lambda y \quad \text{(Eq. 2)} \\ 2(z+1) \amp = 2\lambda z \implies z + 1 = \lambda z \quad \text{(Eq. 3)} \\ x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \amp = 4 \quad \text{(Eq. 4)} \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We isolate the coordinate variables in terms of \(\lambda\) from the first three equations:
    \begin{equation*} x(1 - \lambda) = 3 \implies x = \frac{3}{1-\lambda} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} y(1 - \lambda) = 1 \implies y = \frac{1}{1-\lambda} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} z(1 - \lambda) = -1 \implies z = \frac{-1}{1-\lambda} \end{equation*}
    Notice that the coordinates are proportional to each other: \(x = 3y\) and \(z = -y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We substitute these proportional ratios directly into our sphere constraint (Eq. 4):
    \begin{align*} (3y)^2 + y^2 + (-y)^2 \amp = 4\\ 9y^2 + y^2 + y^2 \amp = 4\\ 11y^2 \amp = 4 \implies y^2 = \frac{4}{11} \implies y = \pm \frac{2}{\sqrt{11}} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Using our proportional ratios, we find the two corresponding sets of coordinates:
    • When \(y = \frac{2}{\sqrt{11}}\text{,}\) we get the point: \(P_1 = \left(\frac{6}{\sqrt{11}}, \, \frac{2}{\sqrt{11}}, \, -\frac{2}{\sqrt{11}}\right)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • When \(y = -\frac{2}{\sqrt{11}}\text{,}\) we get the point: \(P_2 = \left(-\frac{6}{\sqrt{11}}, \, -\frac{2}{\sqrt{11}}, \, \frac{2}{\sqrt{11}}\right)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Geometrically, the point \(P_1\) has positive coordinates that align with the direction toward \((3,1,-1)\text{,}\) making it the closest point. The point \(P_2\) has opposite orientation, making it the farthest point.
    🔗
    Thus, the point \(\left(\frac{6}{\sqrt{11}}, \frac{2}{\sqrt{11}}, -\frac{2}{\sqrt{11}}\right)\) is closest to the given point, and \(\left(-\frac{6}{\sqrt{11}}, -\frac{2}{\sqrt{11}}, \frac{2}{\sqrt{11}}\right)\) is farthest.
