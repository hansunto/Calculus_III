    Section 3.3 Partial Derivatives

    In this section, we will explore the concept of partial derivatives, which are essential for understanding how functions of multiple variables change with respect to each independent variable.
    🔗
    We will begin by defining first-order partial derivatives for functions of two or more variables and establishing their geometric interpretations as slopes of tangent lines along cross-sectional planes. Next, we will extend these concepts to higher-order modifications, uncovering how mixed partial variants operate under Clairaut’s Theorem. Finally, we will apply these techniques to verify solutions to classic multivariate physics and engineering frameworks, including Laplace’s Equation and the Cauchy-Riemann Equations.
    🔗
    By the end of this section, you will have a solid conceptual understanding of multivariate rate of change behaviors and a strong structural foundation for navigating complex partial differential layouts in multivariable calculus.
    🔗

    Definition 3.27.

    Let \(f\) be a function of two variables. The first partial derivatives of \(f\) with respect to \(x\) and \(y\) are the functions \(f_x\) and \(f_y\) defined by the limits:
    \begin{align*} f_x(x,y) \amp = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x+h,y)-f(x,y)}{h}\\ f_y(x,y) \amp = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x,y+h)-f(x,y)}{h} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.28. Notation for Partial Derivatives.

    If \(z=f(x,y)\text{,}\) then the alternative notations for the partial derivatives include:
    \begin{equation*} f_x = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \quad f_y = \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y) = D_x f = f_x = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} f(x,y) = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = z_x \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f_y(x,y) = D_y f = f_y = \frac{\partial}{\partial y} f(x,y) = \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = z_y \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.29.

    If \(f(x,y)=x^3y^2-2x^2y+3x\text{,}\) find \(f_x(2,-1)\) and \(f_y(2,-1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    To find \(f_x(2,-1)\text{,}\) we first find the general partial derivative with respect to \(x\text{,}\) \(f_x(x,y)\text{,}\) by treating \(y\) as a constant:
    \begin{align*} f_x(x,y) \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^3y^2 - 2x^2y + 3x)\\ \amp = 3x^2y^2 - 4xy + 3 \end{align*}
    Now, we substitute \(x = 2\) and \(y = -1\) into our result:
    \begin{align*} f_x(2,-1) \amp = 3(2)^2(-1)^2 - 4(2)(-1) + 3\\ \amp = 3(4)(1) - (-8) + 3\\ \amp = 12 + 8 + 3\\ \amp = 23 \end{align*}
    🔗
    To find \(f_y(2,-1)\text{,}\) we find the general partial derivative with respect to \(y\text{,}\) \(f_y(x,y)\text{,}\) by treating \(x\) as a constant:
    \begin{align*} f_y(x,y) \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^3y^2 - 2x^2y + 3x)\\ \amp = 2x^3y - 2x^2 + 0\\ \amp = 2x^3y - 2x^2 \end{align*}
    Now, we substitute \(x = 2\) and \(y = -1\) into this expression:
    \begin{align*} f_y(2,-1) \amp = 2(2)^3(-1) - 2(2)^2\\ \amp = 2(8)(-1) - 2(4)\\ \amp = -16 - 8\\ \amp = -24 \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.30.

    If \(f(x,y)=4-x^2-2y^2\text{,}\) find \(f_x(1,1)\) and \(f_y(1,1)\text{,}\) and interpret these numbers as slopes.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, we compute the general partial derivatives by treating the opposite variable as a constant:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y) = -2x \quad \text{and} \quad f_y(x,y) = -4y \end{equation*}
    Evaluating these at the point \((1,1)\) yields:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(1,1) = -2 \quad \text{and} \quad f_y(1,1) = -4 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To interpret these numbers geometrically, note that the graph of \(f\) is a paraboloid, and the function value at this point is \(f(1,1) = 4 - 1^2 - 2(1)^2 = 1\text{,}\) giving the point \((1,1,1)\) on the surface.
    🔗
    The vertical plane \(y=1\) intersects the paraboloid in the curve (a parabola) described by \(z = 4 - x^2 - 2(1)^2 = 2 - x^2\) in the plane \(y=1\text{.}\) The slope of the tangent line to this parabola at the point \((1,1,1)\) is given by \(f_x(1,1) = -2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Similarly, the vertical plane \(x=1\) intersects the paraboloid in the curve described by \(z = 4 - (1)^2 - 2y^2 = 3 - 2y^2\) in the plane \(x=1\text{.}\) The slope of the tangent line to this parabola at the point \((1,1,1)\) is given by \(f_y(1,1) = -4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.32.

    If \(f(x,y)=\sin\left(\frac{x}{x+y}\right)\text{,}\) find \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\) and \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Using the Chain Rule for functions of one variable, we differentiate the outer sine function while keeping the inner fraction intact, and then multiply by the partial derivative of the inner expression.
    🔗
    For the partial derivative with respect to \(x\text{,}\) we use the quotient rule on the inner function while treating \(y\) as a constant:
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \amp = \cos\left(\frac{x}{x+y}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial }{\partial x}\left(\frac{x}{x+y}\right)\\ \amp = \cos\left(\frac{x}{x+y}\right) \cdot \frac{(x+y)(1) - x(1)}{(x+y)^2}\\ \amp = \cos\left(\frac{x}{x+y}\right) \cdot \frac{y}{(x+y)^2} \end{align*}
    🔗
    For the partial derivative with respect to \(y\text{,}\) we treat \(x\) as a constant and apply the chain rule/power rule to the inner function:
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \amp = \cos\left(\frac{x}{x+y}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial }{\partial y}\left(\frac{x}{x+y}\right)\\ \amp = \cos\left(\frac{x}{x+y}\right) \cdot \frac{\partial }{\partial y}\left(x(x+y)^{-1}\right)\\ \amp = \cos\left(\frac{x}{x+y}\right) \cdot \left(-x(x+y)^{-2}\right)\\ \amp = -\cos\left(\frac{x}{x+y}\right) \cdot \frac{x}{(x+y)^2} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.33. Functions of More Than Two Variables.

    Let \(f\) be a function of three variables. The first partial derivatives of \(f\) with respect to \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(z\) are the functions \(f_x\text{,}\) \(f_y\text{,}\) and \(f_z\) defined by:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y,z) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x+h,y,z)-f(x,y,z)}{h} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    In general, if \(f\) is a function of \(n\) variables, \(f=f(x_1,x_2,x_3,\dots,x_n)\text{,}\) its partial derivative with respect to the \(i\)th variable \(x_i\) is:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}= f_{x_i} = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(x_1,\dots, x_{i-1},x_i+h,x_{i+1},\dots,x_n)-f(x_1,\dots, x_{i-1},x_i,x_{i+1},\dots,x_n)}{h} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.34.

    If \(f(x,y,z)=x^2y^3\sin z+e^{xy}\ln z\text{,}\) find \(f_x\text{,}\) \(f_y\text{,}\) and \(f_z\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    To find each partial derivative, we differentiate with respect to the chosen variable while holding all other variables constant:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} f_{x} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2) \cdot y^3\sin z + \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(e^{xy}) \cdot \ln z\\ \amp = 2xy^3\sin z + ye^{xy}\ln z \end{align*}
    🔗
    \begin{align*} f_{y} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \amp = x^2 \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y^3) \cdot \sin z + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(e^{xy}) \cdot \ln z\\ \amp = 3x^2y^2\sin z + xe^{xy}\ln z \end{align*}
    🔗
    \begin{align*} f_{z} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} \amp = x^2y^3 \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(\sin z) + e^{xy} \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(\ln z)\\ \amp = x^2y^3\cos z + \frac{e^{xy}}{z} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.35.

    If \(f\) is a function of two variables, then its partial derivatives \(f_x\) and \(f_y\) are also functions of two variables. Consequently, we can consider their partial derivatives \((f_x)_x\text{,}\) \((f_x)_y\text{,}\) \((f_y)_x\text{,}\) and \((f_y)_y\text{,}\) which are called the second partial derivatives of \(f\text{.}\) If \(z=f(x,y)\text{,}\) we denote them as follows:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (f_x)_x = f_{xx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial x^2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (f_x)_y = f_{xy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y\,\partial x} = \frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial y\,\partial x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (f_y)_x = f_{yx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x\,\partial y} = \frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial x\,\partial y} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (f_y)_y = f_{yy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} = \frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial y^2} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 3.36.

    Find the second partial derivatives of
    \begin{equation*} f(x,y)=x^3+x^2y^3-2y^2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    We begin by calculating the first-order partial derivatives of \(f\) with respect to \(x\) and \(y\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^3+x^2y^3-2y^2) = 3x^2 + 2xy^3 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f_y(x,y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^3+x^2y^3-2y^2) = 3x^2y^2 - 4y \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Next, we differentiate \(f_x\) and \(f_y\) to find the four second-order partial derivatives:
    🔗
    1. Differentiating \(f_x\) with respect to \(x\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} f_{xx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(3x^2 + 2xy^3) = 6x + 2y^3 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Differentiating \(f_x\) with respect to \(y\) (mixed partial):
      \begin{equation*} f_{xy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(3x^2 + 2xy^3) = 6xy^2 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Differentiating \(f_y\) with respect to \(x\) (mixed partial):
      \begin{equation*} f_{yx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(3x^2y^2 - 4y) = 6xy^2 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Differentiating \(f_y\) with respect to \(y\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} f_{yy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(3x^2y^2 - 4y) = 6x^2y - 4 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Notice that the mixed partial derivatives are equal (\(f_{xy} = f_{yx} = 6xy^2\)).
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.37. Clairaut’s Theorem (Mixed Derivatives).

    Suppose \(f\) is defined on a disk \(D\) that contains the point \((a,b)\text{.}\) If the functions \(f_{xy}\) and \(f_{yx}\) are both continuous on \(D\text{,}\) then:
    \begin{equation*} f_{xy}(a,b)=f_{yx}(a,b) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.38. Mixed Derivatives.

    Compute \(f_{xxyz}\) if \(f(x,y,z)=\sin(3x+yz)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution 1. Direct Method (Order: \(x, x, y, z\))
    We compute the fourth-order partial derivative step by step, applying the single-variable chain and product rules successively in the order written:
    \begin{align*} f_x \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}[\sin(3x+yz)] = 3\cos(3x+yz)\\ f_{xx} \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}[3\cos(3x+yz)] = -9\sin(3x+yz)\\ f_{xxy} \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}[-9\sin(3x+yz)] = -9z\cos(3x+yz) \end{align*}
    Now, using the single-variable product rule to differentiate \(f_{xxy}\) with respect to \(z\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} f_{xxyz} \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial z}[-9z\cos(3x+yz)]\\ \amp = -9\cos(3x+yz) + (-9z)\cdot\left(-\sin(3x+yz)\cdot y\right)\\ \amp = -9\cos(3x+yz) + 9yz\sin(3x+yz) \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution 2. Alternative Method via Clairaut’s Theorem (Order: \(x, x, z, y\))
    Since the function \(f(x,y,z)\) and all of its partial derivatives are continuous everywhere, we can apply Clairaut’s Theorem to change the order of differentiation to make the algebra simpler. Let’s differentiate with respect to \(z\) before differentiating with respect to \(y\text{,}\) so we evaluate \(f_{xxzy}\) instead of \(f_{xxyz}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} f_{xxyz} = f_{xxzy} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial z} (f_{xx}) \right] \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Using our previous result for the second derivative, \(f_{xx} = -9\sin(3x+yz)\text{,}\) we differentiate with respect to \(z\) first:
    \begin{align*} f_{xxz} \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial z}[-9\sin(3x+yz)]\\ \amp = -9\cos(3x+yz) \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(3x+yz)\\ \amp = -9y\cos(3x+yz) \end{align*}
    🔗
    Finally, we differentiate \(f_{xxz}\) with respect to \(y\text{.}\) Because \(y\) appears both outside and inside the cosine term, we apply the single-variable product rule:
    \begin{align*} f_{xxzy} \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}[-9y\cos(3x+yz)]\\ \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}[-9y] \cdot \cos(3x+yz) + (-9y) \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial y}[\cos(3x+yz)]\\ \amp = -9\cos(3x+yz) + (-9y) \cdot \left( -\sin(3x+yz) \cdot z \right)\\ \amp = -9\cos(3x+yz) + 9yz\sin(3x+yz) \end{align*}
    🔗
    Both mixed partial orders yield the exact same final expression, validating Clairaut’s Theorem.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.39. Harmonic Function.

    If \(u\) is a function of two variables and satisfies the partial differential equation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} = 0 \end{equation*}
    this equation is called Laplace’s equation. Solutions to this equation are called harmonic functions and are frequently used in problems involving heat conduction and fluid flow.
    🔗
    Show that the function \(u(x,y)=e^x \cos(y)\) is harmonic.
    🔗
    Solution.
    To show that \(u(x,y)\) is a harmonic function, we must verify that it satisfies Laplace’s equation by calculating its second-order pure partial derivatives.
    🔗
    First, we find the first and second partial derivatives with respect to \(x\text{,}\) treating \(y\) as a constant:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}[e^x \cos(y)] = e^x \cos(y) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}[e^x \cos(y)] = e^x \cos(y) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Next, we find the first and second partial derivatives with respect to \(y\text{,}\) treating \(x\) as a constant:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}[e^x \cos(y)] = -e^x \sin(y) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}[-e^x \sin(y)] = -e^x \cos(y) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now, we substitute both second-order pure partial derivatives into the left-hand side of Laplace’s equation:
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2} \amp = e^x \cos(y) + \left(-e^x \cos(y)\right)\\ \amp = 0 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Since the sum of the second partial derivatives is identically \(0\) everywhere, the function satisfies Laplace’s equation. Therefore, \(u(x,y) = e^x \cos(y)\) is a harmonic function.

    Example 3.40. Cauchy-Riemann Equation.

    If \(u\) and \(v\) are functions of two variables and satisfy
    \begin{equation*} u_x=v_y \quad \text{and} \quad u_y=-v_x \end{equation*}
    these partial differential equations are called the Cauchy-Riemann equations.
    🔗
    Show that the functions \(u(x,y)=\frac{y}{x^2+y^2}\) and \(v(x,y)=\frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\) satisfy the Cauchy–Riemann equations.
    🔗
    Solution.
    To verify that \(u\) and \(v\) satisfy the Cauchy–Riemann equations, we must compute all four first-order partial derivatives using the single-variable quotient rule.
    🔗
    First, we compute the partial derivatives of \(u(x,y)\) with respect to \(x\) and \(y\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} u_x \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left[\frac{y}{x^2+y^2}\right] = \frac{(x^2+y^2)(0) - y(2x)}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = \frac{-2xy}{(x^2+y^2)^2}\\ u_y \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left[\frac{y}{x^2+y^2}\right] = \frac{(x^2+y^2)(1) - y(2y)}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = \frac{x^2-y^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Next, we compute the partial derivatives of \(v(x,y)\) with respect to \(x\) and \(y\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} v_x \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left[\frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\right] = \frac{(x^2+y^2)(1) - x(2x)}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = \frac{y^2-x^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}\\ v_y \amp = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left[\frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\right] = \frac{(x^2+y^2)(0) - x(2y)}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = \frac{-2xy}{(x^2+y^2)^2} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Now we check both conditions of the Cauchy–Riemann system:
    🔗
    1. Comparing \(u_x\) and \(v_y\text{,}\) we see they match perfectly:
      \begin{equation*} u_x = \frac{-2xy}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = v_y \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Comparing \(u_y\) and \(v_x\text{,}\) we observe they are exact negatives of each other:
      \begin{equation*} -v_x = -\left(\frac{y^2-x^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}\right) = \frac{x^2-y^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = u_y \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Since both partial differential equations hold true on their mutual domain \(\{(x,y) \mid (x,y) \neq (0,0)\}\text{,}\) the functions \(u\) and \(v\) satisfy the Cauchy-Riemann equations.
