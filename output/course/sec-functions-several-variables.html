    Section 3.1 Functions of Several Variables

    In this section, we will introduce the concept of functions of several variables. These functions take multiple independent inputs and produce a single real-number output, allowing us to model complex structural relationships in higher dimensions.
    🔗
    We will explore how to analytically determine the domain and range of these functions, as well as how to interpret their geometric behavior through three-dimensional graphical representations.
    🔗
    Additionally, we will discuss the concept of level curves and level surfaces, which slice through multivariate outputs to provide valuable structural insights into their contours and topologies.
    🔗

    Definition 3.1.

    Let \(D\) be a set of ordered pairs of real variables. A function \(f\) of two variables is a correspondence that assigns to each pair \((x,y)\) in \(D\) exactly one real number denoted \(f(x,y)\text{.}\) The set \(D\) is called the domain of \(f\). The range of \(f\) consists of all real numbers \(f(x,y)\) where \((x,y)\) is in \(D\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.2.

    Find the domain and range of \(g(x,y)=\sqrt{9-x^2-y^2}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The domain of \(g\) is
    \begin{equation*} D=\{(x,y) \mid 9-x^2-y^2\geq 0\}=\{(x,y) \mid x^2+y^2\leq 9\} \end{equation*}
    which is the disk with center \((0,0)\) and radius 3. The range of \(g\) is
    \begin{equation*} \{z \mid z=\sqrt{9-x^2-y^2}, \quad (x,y)\in D \} \end{equation*}
    Since \(z\geq 0\text{,}\) and also \(9-x^2-y^2\leq 9 \implies \sqrt{9-x^2-y^2}\leq 3\) the range of \(g\) is
    \begin{equation*} \{z \mid 0\leq z\leq 3\} \quad = \quad [0,3] \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.3.

    Sketch the graph of \(g(x,y)=\sqrt{9-x^2-y^2}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We squared both sides of the equation to obtain \(x^2+y^2+z^2=9\) for \(z\geq 0\text{.}\) The graph of \(g\) is the upper hemisphere of a sphere centered at the origin with a radius of 3.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.5.

    The level curve of a function \(f\) of two variables are the curves with equations \(f(x,y)=k\) where \(k\) is a constant (in the range of \(f\)).
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.6.

    Sketch some level curves of \(f(x,y)=y^2-x^2\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We know \(z=y^2-x^2\) is a hyperbolic paraboloid and the level curves are \(k=y^2-x^2\) with \(k\in \mathbb{Z}\text{.}\) We get hyperbolas with vertices \((0, \pm \sqrt{k})\) when \(k > 0\text{,}\) vertices \((\pm\sqrt{-k},0)\) when \(k < 0\text{,}\) and the intersecting lines \(y=\pm x\) if \(k=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    A function of three variables \(f\) assigns to each ordered triple \((x,y,z)\) in a domain \(D \subset \mathbb{R}^3\) exactly one real number denoted \(f(x,y,z)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 3.8.

    Find the domain of \(f\) if
    \begin{equation*} f(x,y,z)=\ln(z-y)+xy\sin z \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    The domain of \(f\) is
    \begin{equation*} D = \{(x,y,z)\in \mathbb{R}^3 \mid z-y>0 \} = \{(x,y,z)\in \mathbb{R}^3 \mid z>y \} \end{equation*}
    This is a half-space consisting of all points that lie above the plane \(z=y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.9.

    Find the level surfaces of the function
    \begin{equation*} f(x,y, z)=x^2-y^2+z^2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    The level surfaces are \(x^2-y^2+z^2=k\text{,}\) which form the following families:
    🔗
    1. where \(k> 0\text{.}\) These form a family of hyperboloids of one sheet with axes along the \(y\)-axis.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. where \(k= 0\text{.}\) This forms a cone with its axis along the \(y\)-axis.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. where \(k< 0\text{.}\) These form a family of hyperboloids of two sheets with axes along the \(y\)-axis.
      🔗
      🔗
