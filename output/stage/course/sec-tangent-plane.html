    Section 3.7 Tangent Planes and Normal Lines

    The tangent plane to a surface at a given point is the plane that best approximates the surface near that point. It is defined by the point of tangency and the normal vector to the surface at that point.
    🔗
    Suppose \(S\) is a surface with the equation \(F(x,y,z)=0\text{;}\) that is, it is a level surface of a function \(F\) of three variables. Let \(P(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) be a point on \(S\text{,}\) and let \(C\) be any arbitrary curve that lies completely on the surface \(S\) and passes through the point \(P\text{.}\) The curve \(C\) can be described by a continuous vector function:
    \begin{equation*} \mathbf{r}(t) = \langle f(t), \, g(t), \, h(t) \rangle \end{equation*}
    Let \(t_0\) be the specific parameter value corresponding to the point \(P\text{,}\) such that \(\mathbf{r}(t_0) = \langle x_0, \, y_0, \, z_0 \rangle\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Since the curve \(C\) lies on the surface \(S\text{,}\) the coordinates of any point on the curve must satisfy the structural equation of \(S\text{.}\) This establishes the identity:
    \begin{equation} F(f(t), g(t), h(t)) = 0\tag{1} \end{equation}
    If \(f\text{,}\) \(g\text{,}\) and \(h\) are differentiable functions of \(t\) and \(F\) is also differentiable, we can apply the Multivariate Chain Rule to differentiate both sides of (1) with respect to \(t\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\partial F}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt} + \frac{\partial F}{\partial z}\frac{dz}{dt} = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Recognizing that the components of this sum match the definitions of the gradient vector \(\nabla F = \langle F_x, F_y, F_z \rangle\) and the tangent velocity vector \(\mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle f'(t), g'(t), h'(t) \rangle\text{,}\) we can rewrite this relationship as a clean vector dot product:
    \begin{equation*} \nabla F \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) = 0 \end{equation*}
    In particular, when evaluated at the parameter point \(t=t_0\text{,}\) this becomes:
    \begin{equation*} \nabla F(x_0,y_0,z_0) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t_0) = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This geometric result proves that the gradient vector \(\nabla F\) is perpendicular to the tangent vector \(\mathbf{r}'(t_0)\) of *any* curve \(C\) on \(S\) that passes through \(P\text{.}\) Provided that \(\nabla F(x_0,y_0,z_0) \neq \mathbf{0}\text{,}\) it defines the orthogonal orientation of the surface. We therefore define the tangent plane to the level surface \(F(x,y,z)=0\) at \(P(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) as the plane passing through \(P\) that uses the gradient vector \(\nabla F\) as its normal vector.
    🔗

    Corollary 3.85. Equation of the Tangent Plane.

    An equation for the tangent plane to the level surface \(F(x,y,z)=0\) at the point \(P(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) is given by:
    \begin{equation*} F_x(x_0,y_0,z_0)(x-x_0) + F_y(x_0,y_0,z_0)(y-y_0) + F_z(x_0,y_0,z_0)(z-z_0) = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.86.

    Find an equation for the tangent plane to the ellipsoid \(\frac{3}{4}x^2+3y^2+z^2=12\) at the point \(P_0(2,1,\sqrt{6})\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We begin by moving all terms to one side of the equation to define our three-variable level surface identity function, \(F(x,y,z) = 0\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} F(x,y,z) = \frac{3}{4}x^2 + 3y^2 + z^2 - 12 = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    According to the Tangent Plane Theorem, the components of the normal vector to the plane are given by the gradient vector \(\nabla F\) evaluated at the point \(P_0\text{.}\) We calculate the three first-order partial derivatives of \(F\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} F_x(x,y,z) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{3}{4}x^2 + 3y^2 + z^2 - 12\right) = \frac{3}{2}x \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} F_y(x,y,z) = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{3}{4}x^2 + 3y^2 + z^2 - 12\right) = 6y \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} F_z(x,y,z) = \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\left(\frac{3}{4}x^2 + 3y^2 + z^2 - 12\right) = 2z \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now, we evaluate these partial derivatives specifically at the point \(P_0(2,1,\sqrt{6})\) to find our normal vector components:
    \begin{equation*} F_x(2,1,\sqrt{6}) = \frac{3}{2}(2) = 3 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} F_y(2,1,\sqrt{6}) = 6(1) = 6 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} F_z(2,1,\sqrt{6}) = 2(\sqrt{6}) = 2\sqrt{6} \end{equation*}
    This yields the orthogonal normal gradient vector: \(\nabla F(2,1,\sqrt{6}) = \langle 3, \, 6, \, 2\sqrt{6} \rangle\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Applying the Corollary for the equation of a tangent plane, we substitute our point coordinates and normal components:
    \begin{equation*} F_x(P_0)(x - x_0) + F_y(P_0)(y - y_0) + F_z(P_0)(z - z_0) = 0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 3(x - 2) + 6(y - 1) + 2\sqrt{6}(z - \sqrt{6}) = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Finally, we expand and simplify the linear algebraic equation:
    \begin{align*} 3x - 6 + 6y - 6 + 2\sqrt{6}z - 2(6) \amp = 0\\ 3x + 6y + 2\sqrt{6}z - 6 - 6 - 12 \amp = 0\\ 3x + 6y + 2\sqrt{6}z - 24 \amp = 0\\ 3x + 6y + 2\sqrt{6}z \amp = 24 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Thus, the equation for the tangent plane to the ellipsoid at the given point is \(3x + 6y + 2\sqrt{6}z = 24\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.87. Tangent Plane to an Explicit Surface.

    An equation for the tangent plane to the graph of the explicit surface \(z=f(x,y)\) at the point \(P_0(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) is given by:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x_0,y_0)(x-x_0) + f_y(x_0,y_0)(y-y_0) = z-z_0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The normal line to a level surface \(S\) at a point \(P_0(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) is the line passing through \(P_0\) that is perpendicular to the tangent plane. Because the gradient vector \(\nabla F(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) acts as the orthogonal normal vector to the tangent plane, it serves directly as the direction vector for this line. Thus, the symmetric equations of the normal line are:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x-x_0}{F_x(x_0,y_0,z_0)} = \frac{y-y_0}{F_y(x_0,y_0,z_0)} = \frac{z-z_0}{F_z(x_0,y_0,z_0)} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 3.88.

    Find an equation for the normal line to the ellipsoid \(\frac{3}{4}x^2+3y^2+z^2=12\) at the point \(P_0(2,1,\sqrt{6})\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We define our level surface function as \(F(x,y,z) = \frac{3}{4}x^2 + 3y^2 + z^2 - 12 = 0\text{.}\) From our previous calculations, the components of the gradient vector evaluated at \(P_0(2,1,\sqrt{6stream})\) are:
    \begin{equation*} F_x(P_0) = 3, \quad F_y(P_0) = 6, \quad F_z(P_0) = 2\sqrt{6} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Using these partial derivatives as directional components, we substitute them along with the coordinates of \(P_0\) into the symmetric equation formula:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x-2}{3} = \frac{y-1}{6} = \frac{z-\sqrt{6}}{2\sqrt{6}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.89.

    Let \(P_0\) be the point \((3,-4,2)\) on the hyperboloid \(16x^2-9y^2+36z^2=144\text{.}\) Find equations for both the tangent plane and the normal line at \(P_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, we move all terms to one side to define our three-variable identity function:
    \begin{equation*} F(x,y,z) = 16x^2 - 9y^2 + 36z^2 - 144 = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Next, we calculate the three first-order partial derivatives of \(F\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} F_x(x,y,z) = 32x, \quad F_y(x,y,z) = -18y, \quad F_z(x,y,z) = 72z \end{equation*}
    Evaluating these derivatives at the given point \(P_0(3,-4,2)\) yields our normal vector components:
    \begin{equation*} F_x(3,-4,2) = 32(3) = 96 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} F_y(3,-4,2) = -18(-4) = 72 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} F_z(3,-4,2) = 72(2) = 144 \end{equation*}
    This gives the gradient vector \(\nabla F(3,-4,2) = \langle 96, 72, 144 \rangle\text{.}\) We can factor out a common multiple of 24 to obtain a simplified parallel normal vector direction: \(\langle 4, 3, 6 \rangle\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To find the equation of the tangent plane, we apply the point-normal equation:
    \begin{equation*} 96(x - 3) + 72(y - (-4)) + 144(z - 2) = 0 \end{equation*}
    Dividing the entire equation by 24 simplifies the coefficients:
    \begin{align*} 4(x - 3) + 3(y + 4) + 6(z - 2) \amp = 0\\ 4x - 12 + 3y + 12 + 6z - 12 \amp = 0\\ 4x + 3y + 6z \amp = 12 \end{align*}
    🔗
    To find the symmetric equations for the normal line, we use the same evaluated partial derivative denominators and point coordinates:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x-3}{4} = \frac{y+4}{3} = \frac{z-2}{6} \end{equation*}
