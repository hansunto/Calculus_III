    Section 1.1 Vectors in Two Dimensions

    A vector is a quantity that has both magnitude and direction. In two dimensions, vectors can be represented as arrows in a plane, where the length of the arrow corresponds to the magnitude of the vector, and the direction of the arrow indicates the direction of the vector.
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.

    A velocity or force has both magnitude and direction and is often represented by a directed line segment or a vector. We denote the vector with initial point \(P\) and terminal point \(Q\) as \(\vec{PQ}\text{.}\) The direction of the vector indicates arrowhead at \(Q\text{.}\) The magnitude of \(\vec{PQ}\) is the length of the segment \(PQ\) and is denoted by \(\|\vec{PQ}\|\text{.}\) Vectors that have the same magnitude and direction are called equivalent.
    🔗
    🔗
    A vector may be translated from one location to another, provided neither the magnitude nor the direction is changed. If \(\vec{PS} = \vec{PQ} + \vec{PR}\) and \(\vec{PQ}\) and \(\vec{PR}\) are two forces acting at \(P\text{,}\) then \(\vec{PS}\) is the resultant force (sum).
    🔗
    If \(c\) is a scalar and \(\vec{v}\) is a vector, then \(c\vec{v}\) (a scalar multiple) is defined as a vector whose magnitude is \(|c|\) times the magnitude \(\|\vec{v}\|\) of \(\vec{v}\) with the same direction (\(c \gt 0\)) or the opposite direction (\(c \lt 0\)) of \(\vec{v}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    A vector with the initial point at the origin is called the position vector. We use the symbol \(\langle a_1, a_2 \rangle\) for an ordered pair that represents the vector and denote it by a lowercase letter \(\vec{v} = \langle a_1, a_2 \rangle\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Two vectors \(\langle a_1, a_2 \rangle\) and \(\langle b_1, b_2 \rangle\) are equal \(\iff a_1 = b_1\) and \(a_2 = b_2\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 1.4.

    The magnitude (or length) \(\|\vec{a}\|\) of the vector \(\vec{a} = \langle a_1,a_2 \rangle\) is
    \begin{equation*} \|\vec{a}\| = \|\langle a_1,a_2 \rangle\| = \sqrt{a_1^2+a_2^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.5.

    Find the magnitude of each vector:
    🔗
    1. \(\displaystyle \vec{a} = \langle -3,2 \rangle\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \vec{b} = \langle 0,-2 \rangle\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\displaystyle \vec{c} = \left\langle \frac{4}{5},\frac{3}{5} \right\rangle\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    We apply the magnitude formula \(\|\vec{v}\| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2}\) to each vector:
    🔗
    1. For \(\vec{a} = \langle -3,2 \rangle\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \|\vec{a}\| = \sqrt{(-3)^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{9 + 4} = \sqrt{13} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. For \(\vec{b} = \langle 0,-2 \rangle\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \|\vec{b}\| = \sqrt{0^2 + (-2)^2} = \sqrt{0 + 4} = \sqrt{4} = 2 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. For \(\vec{c} = \left\langle \frac{4}{5},\frac{3}{5} \right\rangle\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \|\vec{c}\| = \sqrt{\left(\frac{4}{5}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{3}{5}\right)^2} = \sqrt{\frac{16}{25} + \frac{9}{25}} = \sqrt{\frac{25}{25}} = 1 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Because its magnitude is \(1\text{,}\) \(\vec{c}\) is a unit vector.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Fact 1.6. Vector Operations.

    Addition of vectors
    \begin{equation*} \langle a_1,a_2 \rangle + \langle b_1,b_2 \rangle = \langle a_1+b_1, a_2+b_2 \rangle \end{equation*}
    Scalar multiple of a vector
    \begin{equation*} c \langle a_1,a_2 \rangle = \langle ca_1, ca_2 \rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.9.

    The zero vector 0 is defined as \(\vec{0} = \langle 0,0 \rangle\text{,}\) and the negative -a of a vector \(\vec{a} = \langle a_1,a_2 \rangle\) is defined as:
    \begin{equation*} -\vec{a} = -\langle a_1,a_2 \rangle = \langle -a_1,-a_2 \rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.10. Properties of Vectors.

    Let \(\vec{a}\text{,}\) \(\vec{b}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{c}\) be vectors, and let \(c\) and \(d\) be scalars. Then:
    🔗
    1. \(\vec{a}+\vec{b}=\vec{b}+\vec{a}\text{,}\) and \(c(\vec{a}+\vec{b})=c\vec{a}+c\vec{b}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \vec{a}+(\vec{b}+\vec{c})=(\vec{a}+\vec{b})+\vec{c}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\vec{a}+\vec{0}=\vec{a}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{a}+(-\vec{a})=\vec{0}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \((c+d)\vec{a}=c\vec{a}+d\vec{a}\text{,}\) and \((cd)\vec{a}=c(d\vec{a})=d(c\vec{a})\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. \(1\vec{a}=\vec{a}\text{,}\) and \(0\vec{a}=\vec{0}=c\vec{0}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.11.

    Let \(\vec{a}=\langle a_1,a_2 \rangle\) and \(\vec{b}=\langle b_1,b_2 \rangle\text{.}\) The difference a-b is:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a}-\vec{b}=\vec{a}+(-\vec{b}) = \langle a_1-b_1, a_2-b_2 \rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.12.

    If \(\vec{a}=\langle 5,-3 \rangle\) and \(\vec{b}=\langle -2,3 \rangle\text{,}\) find:
    🔗
    1. \(\displaystyle \vec{a}-\vec{b}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle 3\vec{a}-2\vec{b}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. To find \(\vec{a}-\vec{b}\text{,}\) subtract the components of \(\vec{b}\) from the components of \(\vec{a}\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \vec{a}-\vec{b} = \langle 5 - (-2), -3 - 3 \rangle = \langle 5 + 2, -6 \rangle = \langle 7,-6 \rangle \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. To find \(3\vec{a}-2\vec{b}\text{,}\) perform the scalar multiplications first, then subtract:
      \begin{equation*} 3\vec{a} = 3\langle 5,-3 \rangle = \langle 15,-9 \rangle \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} 2\vec{b} = 2\langle -2,3 \rangle = \langle -4,6 \rangle \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Now subtract the resulting components:
      \begin{equation*} 3\vec{a}-2\vec{b} = \langle 15 - (-4), -9 - 6 \rangle = \langle 15 + 4, -15 \rangle = \langle 19,-15 \rangle \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.13. Vector from Two Points.

    If \(P_1(x_1,y_1)\) and \(P_2(x_2,y_2)\) are any two points, the vector \(\vec{a}\) in \(V_2\) that corresponds to the directed line segment \(\vec{P_1P_2}\) is:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} = \langle x_2-x_1, y_2-y_1 \rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.17.

    Nonzero vectors \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) in \(V_2\) have:
    🔗
    1. the same direction if \(\vec{b}=c\vec{a}\) for some scalar \(c \gt 0\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. the opposite direction if \(\vec{b}=c\vec{a}\) for some scalar \(c \lt 0\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.18. Magnitude of a Scalar Multiple.

    If \(\vec{a}\) is a vector and \(c\) is a scalar, then \(\|c\vec{a}\| = |c|\|\vec{a}\|\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.19.

    A vector of length 1 is called a unit vector. The standard unit vectors are:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{i} = \langle 1,0 \rangle, \quad \vec{j} = \langle 0,1 \rangle \end{equation*}
    If \(\vec{a} = \langle a_1,a_2 \rangle\text{,}\) then it can be written as a linear combination of the standard unit vectors:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} = a_1\vec{i} + a_2\vec{j} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.20.

    If \(\vec{a} = -5\vec{i} + 2\vec{j}\) and \(\vec{b} = \vec{i} - 3\vec{j}\text{,}\) express \(2\vec{a} - 3\vec{b}\) as a linear combination of \(\vec{i}\) and \(\vec{j}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Substitute the linear combinations for \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) into the expression, distribute the scalars, and combine like terms:
    \begin{align*} 2\vec{a} - 3\vec{b} \amp = 2(-5\vec{i} + 2\vec{j}) - 3(\vec{i} - 3\vec{j})\\ \amp = (-10\vec{i} + 4\vec{j}) - (3\vec{i} - 9\vec{j})\\ \amp = -10\vec{i} - 3\vec{i} + 4\vec{j} + 9\vec{j}\\ \amp = -13\vec{i} + 13\vec{j} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.21. Unit Vector in the Same Direction.

    A unit vector is a vector of magnitude 1. The vectors \(\vec{i}\) and \(\vec{j}\) are unit vectors. If \(\vec{a} \neq \vec{0}\text{,}\) then the unit vector \(\vec{u}\) that has the same direction as \(\vec{a}\) is:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{u} = \frac{1}{\|\vec{a}\|} \vec{a} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.22.

    Find the unit vector \(\vec{u}\) that has the same direction as \(\vec{a} = -8\vec{i} + 15\vec{j}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, find the magnitude of vector \(\vec{a}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \|\vec{a}\| = \sqrt{(-8)^2 + 15^2} = \sqrt{64 + 225} = \sqrt{289} = 17 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Next, multiply the reciprocal of the magnitude by the original vector \(\vec{a}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \vec{u} = \frac{1}{17}(-8\vec{i} + 15\vec{j}) = -\frac{8}{17}\vec{i} + \frac{15}{17}\vec{j} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.23.

    A quarterback releases the football with a velocity of 50 ft/sec at an angle of \(35^\circ\) with the horizontal. Approximate the horizontal and vertical components of the vector.
    🔗
    Solution.
    A vector \(\vec{v}\) given a magnitude \(\|\vec{v}\|\) and a direction angle \(\theta\) can be written in component form as \(\vec{v} = \langle \|\vec{v}\|\cos(\theta), \|\vec{v}\|\sin(\theta) \rangle\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Given \(\|\vec{v}\| = 50\) and \(\theta = 35^\circ\text{:}\)
    • Horizontal component: \(50\cos(35^\circ) \approx 40.96\text{ ft/sec}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Vertical component: \(50\sin(35^\circ) \approx 28.68\text{ ft/sec}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    In component form, the velocity vector is approximately \(\langle 40.96, 28.68 \rangle\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.24.

    A jet airplane approaches a runway at an angle of \(7.5^\circ\) with the horizontal, traveling at a velocity of 160 mi/hr. Approximate the horizontal and vertical components of the vector.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Given a magnitude of \(\|\vec{v}\| = 160\) and an approach angle of \(\theta = 7.5^\circ\text{:}\)
    • Horizontal component: \(160\cos(7.5^\circ) \approx 158.63\text{ mi/hr}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Vertical component: \(160\sin(7.5^\circ) \approx 20.88\text{ mi/hr}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    In component form, the velocity vector is approximately \(\langle 158.63, 20.88 \rangle\text{.}\) Note that because the plane is descending, the vertical velocity component can be interpreted as acting in the downward direction (\(-20.88\)).
