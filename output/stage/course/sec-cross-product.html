    Section 1.4 The Cross Product (The vector product)

    The cross product of two vectors in three-dimensional space is a vector that is perpendicular to both of the original vectors. It is defined as:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \|\vec{a}\| \|\vec{b}\| \sin(\theta) \vec{n} \end{equation*}
    where \(\theta\) is the angle between the vectors and \(\vec{n}\) is the unit vector perpendicular to both \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 1.64.

    If \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) are nonzero three-dimensional vectors, the cross product of \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) is the vector:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} \times \vec{b} = (\|\vec{a}\| \|\vec{b}\| \sin(\theta))\vec{n} \end{equation*}
    where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) such that \(0 \leq \theta \leq \pi\text{,}\) and \(\vec{n}\) is a unit vector perpendicular to both \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\text{.}\) The direction of \(\vec{n}\) is determined by the right-hand rule: if the fingers of your right hand curl through the angle \(\theta\) from \(\vec{a}\) to \(\vec{b}\text{,}\) then your thumb points in the direction of \(\vec{n}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 1.66.

    The vector \(\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\) points in the same direction as \(\vec{n}\) because it is a scalar multiple of \(\vec{n}\) with a non-negative scalar coefficient. Consequently, \(\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\) is orthogonal to both \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\text{.}\) This is equivalent to stating:
    \begin{equation*} (\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{a} = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad (\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{b} = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    By definition, the cross product is a scalar multiple of the vector \(\vec{n}\text{,}\) meaning \(\vec{a} \times \vec{b} = c\vec{n}\) where \(c = \|\vec{a}\| \|\vec{b}\| \sin(\theta)\text{.}\) Because \(\vec{n}\) is explicitly defined as a unit vector perpendicular to both \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\text{,}\) its dot products with them must evaluate to zero:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{n} \cdot \vec{a} = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \vec{n} \cdot \vec{b} = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Using the scalar distribution properties of the dot product, we can substitute \(c\vec{n}\) back into the orthogonality checks:
    \begin{equation*} (\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{a} = (c\vec{n}) \cdot \vec{a} = c(\vec{n} \cdot \vec{a}) = c(0) = 0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} (\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{b} = (c\vec{n}) \cdot \vec{b} = c(\vec{n} \cdot \vec{b}) = c(0) = 0 \end{equation*}
    This confirms that the resulting vector product remains perfectly perpendicular to both original structural components.
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 1.67.

    Two nonzero vectors \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) are parallel if and only if the angle between them is either \(0\) or \(\pi\text{.}\) Therefore, two nonzero vectors \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) are parallel if and only if:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \vec{0} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    The magnitude of the cross product vector is given by \(\|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\| = \|\vec{a}\| \|\vec{b}\| \sin(\theta)\text{.}\) For any vector, its overall value equals the zero vector (\(\vec{0}\)) if and only if its absolute magnitude length scales to exactly \(0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Because we are evaluating two strictly nonzero vectors, their isolated scalar magnitudes are positive (\(\|\vec{a}\| \neq 0\) and \(\|\vec{b}\| \neq 0\)). Therefore, the product evaluates to zero if and only if the trigonometric term vanishes:
    \begin{equation*} \|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\| = 0 \iff \sin(\theta) = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    On the restricted tracking interval \(0 \leq \theta \leq \pi\text{,}\) the condition \(\sin(\theta) = 0\) is satisfied if and only if \(\theta = 0\) or \(\theta = \pi\text{.}\) Geometrically, an angle of \(0\) means the vectors point in the exact same direction, and an angle of \(\pi\) means they point in exactly opposite directions. In either case, the vectors are parallel.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.68.

    Find \(\vec{i} \times \vec{j}\) and \(\vec{j} \times \vec{i}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We use the geometric definition of the cross product, \(\vec{a} \times \vec{b} = (\|\vec{a}\| \|\vec{b}\| \sin(\theta))\vec{n}\text{:}\)
    🔗
    • For \(\vec{i} \times \vec{j}\text{,}\) both vectors are unit vectors, so \(\|\vec{i}\| = 1\) and \(\|\vec{j}\| = 1\text{.}\) The standard basis vectors are mutually perpendicular, meaning the angle between them is \(\theta = \pi/2\text{.}\) By the right-hand rule, curling your fingers from the positive \(x\)-axis (\(\vec{i}\)) to the positive \(y\)-axis (\(\vec{j}\)) forces your thumb to point along the positive \(z\)-axis (\(\vec{k}\)):
      \begin{equation*} \vec{i} \times \vec{j} = (1 \cdot 1 \cdot \sin(\pi/2))\vec{k} = (1 \cdot 1 \cdot 1)\vec{k} = \vec{k} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • For \(\vec{j} \times \vec{i}\text{,}\) reversing the order of operations reverses the orientation of your hand gesture. The thumb now points down along the negative \(z\)-axis (\(-\vec{k}\)):
      \begin{equation*} \vec{j} \times \vec{i} = (1 \cdot 1 \cdot \sin(\pi/2))(-\vec{k}) = -\vec{k} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.69.

    The cross product of any vector with itself is always the zero vector, because the angle between identical direction paths is \(\theta = 0\text{,}\) and \(\sin(0) = 0\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \vec{i} \times \vec{i} = \vec{0}, \quad \vec{j} \times \vec{j} = \vec{0}, \quad \vec{k} \times \vec{k} = \vec{0} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection The Cross Product in Component Form

    Fact 1.70. Algebraic Cross Product Formula.

    The cross product of \(\vec{a} = \langle a_1,a_2,a_3 \rangle\) and \(\vec{b} = \langle b_1,b_2,b_3 \rangle\) is:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \langle a_2b_3-a_3b_2, \, a_3b_1-a_1b_3, \, a_1b_2-a_2b_1 \rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The expression for \(\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\) is easier to remember in determinant form. The cross product of \(\vec{a} = a_1\vec{i} + a_2\vec{j} + a_3\vec{k}\) and \(\vec{b} = b_1\vec{i} + b_2\vec{j} + b_3\vec{k}\) can be expanded along its top row as:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \begin{vmatrix} a_2 \amp a_3 \\ b_2 \amp b_3 \end{vmatrix}\vec{i} - \begin{vmatrix} a_1 \amp a_3 \\ b_1 \amp b_3 \end{vmatrix}\vec{j} + \begin{vmatrix} a_1 \amp a_2 \\ b_1 \amp b_2 \end{vmatrix}\vec{k} \end{equation*}
    We compact this row-expansion memory tool by writing it as a formal \(3 \times 3\) matrix determinant:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \begin{vmatrix} \vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ a_1 \amp a_2 \amp a_3 \\ b_1 \amp b_2 \amp b_3 \end{vmatrix} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proposition 1.71. Properties of the Cross Product.

    If \(\vec{a}\text{,}\) \(\vec{b}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{c}\) are vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^3\) and \(c\) is a scalar, then:
    🔗
    1. \(\vec{a} \times \vec{b} = -(\vec{b} \times \vec{a})\) (Anti-commutativity)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle (c\vec{a}) \times \vec{b} = c(\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) = \vec{a} \times (c\vec{b})\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\vec{a} \times (\vec{b} + \vec{c}) = \vec{a} \times \vec{b} + \vec{a} \times \vec{c}\) (Left distributivity)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \((\vec{a} + \vec{b}) \times \vec{c} = \vec{a} \times \vec{c} + \vec{b} \times \vec{c}\) (Right distributivity)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    Let \(\vec{a} = \langle a_1, a_2, a_3 \rangle\text{,}\) \(\vec{b} = \langle b_1, b_2, b_3 \rangle\text{,}\) and \(\vec{c} = \langle c_1, c_2, c_3 \rangle\text{.}\) By algebraic definition, the cross product component vector expansion is:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \langle a_2b_3 - a_3b_2, \, a_3b_1 - a_1b_3, \, a_1b_2 - a_2b_1 \rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    1. Expanding the reverse cross product \(\vec{b} \times \vec{a}\) yields:
      \begin{equation*} \vec{b} \times \vec{a} = \langle b_2a_3 - b_3a_2, \, b_3a_1 - b_1a_3, \, b_1a_2 - b_2a_1 \rangle \end{equation*}
      Factoring out a negative \(-1\) from each scalar entry directly flips the interior subtraction ordering:
      \begin{align*} \vec{b} \times \vec{a} \amp = \langle -(a_2b_3 - a_3b_2), \, -(a_3b_1 - a_1b_3), \, -(a_1b_2 - a_2b_1) \rangle\\ \amp = - \langle a_2b_3 - a_3b_2, \, a_3b_1 - a_1b_3, \, a_1b_2 - a_2b_1 \rangle = -(\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \end{align*}
      Multiplying both sides by \(-1\) satisfies the anti-commutative condition \(\vec{a} \times \vec{b} = -(\vec{b} \times \vec{a})\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Multiplying vector \(\vec{a}\) by a scalar \(c\) updates our definition to \(c\vec{a} = \langle ca_1, ca_2, ca_3 \rangle\text{.}\) Substituting this scalar into the expansion gives:
      \begin{align*} (c\vec{a}) \times \vec{b} \amp = \langle (ca_2)b_3 - (ca_3)b_2, \, (ca_3)b_1 - (ca_1)b_3, \, (ca_1)b_2 - (ca_2)b_1 \rangle\\ \amp = \langle c(a_2b_3 - a_3b_2), \, c(a_3b_1 - a_1b_3), \, c(a_1b_2 - a_2b_1) \rangle\\ \amp = c \langle a_2b_3 - a_3b_2, \, a_3b_1 - a_1b_3, \, a_1b_2 - a_2b_1 \rangle = c(\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \end{align*}
      A symmetric factoring loop shows this expression is identically equal to \(\vec{a} \times (c\vec{b})\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. First, compile the vector addition term: \(\vec{b} + \vec{c} = \langle b_1+c_1, b_2+c_2, b_3+c_3 \rangle\text{.}\) Crossing \(\vec{a}\) with this combined vector yields:
      \begin{align*} \vec{a} \times (\vec{b} + \vec{c}) \amp = \langle a_2(b_3+c_3) - a_3(b_2+c_2), \, a_3(b_1+c_1) - a_1(b_3+c_3), \, a_1(b_2+c_2) - a_2(b_1+c_1) \rangle\\ \amp = \langle a_2b_3 + a_2c_3 - a_3b_2 - a_3c_2, \, a_3b_1 + a_3c_1 - a_1b_3 - a_1c_3, \, a_1b_2 + a_1c_2 - a_2b_1 - a_2c_1 \rangle \end{align*}
      Regrouping the entries splits the single vector back into a clean linear addition step:
      \begin{align*} \amp = \langle (a_2b_3 - a_3b_2) + (a_2c_3 - a_3c_2), \, (a_3b_1 - a_1b_3) + (a_3c_1 - a_1c_3), \, (a_1b_2 - a_2b_1) + (a_1c_2 - a_2c_1) \rangle\\ \amp = \langle a_2b_3 - a_3b_2, \, a_3b_1 - a_1b_3, \, a_1b_2 - a_2b_1 \rangle + \langle a_2c_3 - a_3c_2, \, a_3c_1 - a_1c_3, \, a_1c_2 - a_2c_1 \rangle\\ \amp = \vec{a} \times \vec{b} + \vec{a} \times \vec{c} \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Property 4 can be proven directly by using the anti-commutative law established in step 1, combined with the left distributive property from step 3:
      \begin{align*} (\vec{a} + \vec{b}) \times \vec{c} \amp = -(\vec{c} \times (\vec{a} + \vec{b}))\\ \amp = -(\vec{c} \times \vec{a} + \vec{c} \times \vec{b})\\ \amp = -(\vec{c} \times \vec{a}) - (\vec{c} \times \vec{b})\\ \amp = (\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) + (\vec{b} \times \vec{c}) \end{align*}
      This completes the verification of all four arithmetic identities.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    If \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) are represented by directed line segments with the same initial point, they determine a parallelogram. This parallelogram has a base of length \(\|\vec{a}\|\) and an altitude height of \(\|\vec{b}\|\sin(\theta)\text{.}\) Therefore, the total area \(A\) is:
    \begin{equation*} A = \|\vec{a}\|\|\vec{b}\|\sin(\theta) = \|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\| \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Remark 1.72.

    The length of the cross product vector \(\|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\|\) is equal to the area of the parallelogram determined by the vectors \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.73.

    Find the cross product \(\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\) for each of the following cases:
    🔗
    1. \(\vec{a} = \langle 2,-3,4 \rangle\text{,}\) \(\vec{b} = \langle 6,2,-\frac{1}{2} \rangle\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\vec{a} = 4\vec{j}-3\vec{k}\text{,}\) \(\vec{b} = 2\vec{i}+4\vec{j}+6\vec{k}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\|\vec{a}\| = 6\text{,}\) \(\|\vec{b}\| = 5\text{,}\) and the angle between \(\vec{a}\) and \(\vec{b}\) is \(2\pi/3\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. Set up and evaluate the \(3 \times 3\) determinant:
      \begin{align*} \vec{a} \times \vec{b} \amp = \begin{vmatrix} \vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ 2 \amp -3 \amp 4 \\ 6 \amp 2 \amp -\frac{1}{2} \end{vmatrix}\\ \amp = \begin{vmatrix} -3 \amp 4 \\ 2 \amp -\frac{1}{2} \end{vmatrix}\vec{i} - \begin{vmatrix} 2 \amp 4 \\ 6 \amp -\frac{1}{2} \end{vmatrix}\vec{j} + \begin{vmatrix} 2 \amp -3 \\ 6 \amp 2 \end{vmatrix}\vec{k}\\ \amp = \left( \frac{3}{2} - 8 \right)\vec{i} - (-1 - 24)\vec{j} + (4 - (-18))\vec{k}\\ \amp = -\frac{13}{2}\vec{i} + 25\vec{j} + 22\vec{k} = \left\langle -\frac{13}{2}, 25, 22 \right\rangle \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Write \(\vec{a}\) in full component form as \(\langle 0,4,-3 \rangle\text{:}\)
      \begin{align*} \vec{a} \times \vec{b} \amp = \begin{vmatrix} \vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ 0 \amp 4 \amp -3 \\ 2 \amp 4 \amp 6 \end{vmatrix}\\ \amp = (24 - (-12))\vec{i} - (0 - (-6))\vec{j} + (0 - 8)\vec{k}\\ \amp = 36\vec{i} - 6\vec{j} - 8\vec{k} = \langle 36, -6, -8 \rangle \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Because individual components are missing but the angle is given, we find the magnitude using the geometric formula and label the unit normal directional vector \(\vec{n}\text{:}\)
      \begin{equation*} \|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}\| = \|\vec{a}\| \|\vec{b}\| \sin(\theta) = (6)(5)\sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) = 30\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right) = 15\sqrt{3} \end{equation*}
      Thus, the cross product vector is \(15\sqrt{3}\vec{n}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.74.

    Find a vector perpendicular to the plane that passes through the points \(P(1,4,6)\text{,}\) \(Q(-2,5,-1)\text{,}\) and \(R(1,-1,1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, construct two independent vectors lying inside the plane using the given vertices:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{PQ} = \langle -2 - 1, 5 - 4, -1 - 6 \rangle = \langle -3, 1, -7 \rangle \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \vec{PR} = \langle 1 - 1, -1 - 4, 1 - 6 \rangle = \langle 0, -5, -5 \rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    A vector perpendicular to the plane must be orthogonal to both paths. Compute their cross product:
    \begin{align*} \vec{PQ} \times \vec{PR} \amp = \begin{vmatrix} \vec{i} \amp \vec{j} \amp \vec{k} \\ -3 \amp 1 \amp -7 \\ 0 \amp -5 \amp -5 \end{vmatrix}\\ \amp = (-5 - 35)\vec{i} - (15 - 0)\vec{j} + (15 - 0)\vec{k}\\ \amp = -40\vec{i} - 15\vec{j} + 15\vec{k} = \langle -40, -15, 15 \rangle \end{align*}
    Any non-zero scalar multiple of this vector (such as \(\langle 8, 3, -3 \rangle\) obtained by dividing by \(-5\)) is also perpendicular to the plane.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.75.

    Find the area of the triangle with vertices \(P(1,4,6)\text{,}\) \(Q(-2,5,-1)\text{,}\) and \(R(1,-1,1)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    The area of a triangle is exactly half the area of the parallelogram spanned by vectors sharing vertex \(P\text{.}\) From the previous example, we know \(\vec{PQ} \times \vec{PR} = \langle -40, -15, 15 \rangle\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Compute the magnitude length of this cross product vector:
    \begin{align*} \|\vec{PQ} \times \vec{PR}\| \amp = \sqrt{(-40)^2 + (-15)^2 + 15^2}\\ \amp = \sqrt{1600 + 225 + 225} = \sqrt{2050} = 5\sqrt{82} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Therefore, the area of the triangle is:
    \begin{equation*} \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}\|\vec{PQ} \times \vec{PR}\| = \frac{5\sqrt{82}}{2} \approx 22.64 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.76.

    The product \(\vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} \times \vec{c})\) is called the scalar triple product of the vectors \(\vec{a}\text{,}\) \(\vec{b}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{c}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The area of the base parallelogram is \(A = \|\vec{b} \times \vec{c}\|\text{.}\) If \(\theta\) is the angle between vector \(\vec{a}\) and the cross product vector \(\vec{b} \times \vec{c}\text{,}\) then the slanted vertical height \(h\) of the surrounding three-dimensional parallelepiped is \(h = \|\vec{a}\| |\cos(\theta)|\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Fact 1.77. Volume of a Parallelepiped.

    The volume of the parallelepiped determined by the vectors \(\vec{a}\text{,}\) \(\vec{b}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{c}\) is the absolute magnitude of their scalar triple product:
    \begin{equation*} V = |\vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} \times \vec{c})| = |(\vec{a} \times \vec{b}) \cdot \vec{c}| \end{equation*}
    We can compute this product directly using a single matrix determinant:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} \times \vec{c}) = \begin{vmatrix} a_1 \amp a_2 \amp a_3 \\ b_1 \amp b_2 \amp b_3 \\ c_1 \amp c_2 \amp c_3 \end{vmatrix} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.78.

    Use the scalar triple product to show that the vectors \(\vec{a} = \langle 1,4,-7 \rangle\text{,}\) \(\vec{b} = \langle 2,-1,4 \rangle\text{,}\) and \(\vec{c} = \langle 0,-9,18 \rangle\) are coplanar, meaning they lie in the same plane.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Three vectors are coplanar if and only if the volume of the parallelepiped they span is exactly zero (\(V = 0\)). We check this condition by computing their scalar triple product determinant:
    \begin{align*} \vec{a} \cdot (\vec{b} \times \vec{c}) \amp = \begin{vmatrix} 1 \amp 4 \amp -7 \\ 2 \amp -1 \amp 4 \\ 0 \amp -9 \amp 18 \end{vmatrix}\\ \amp = 1 \begin{vmatrix} -1 \amp 4 \\ -9 \amp 18 \end{vmatrix} - 4 \begin{vmatrix} 2 \amp 4 \\ 0 \amp 18 \end{vmatrix} + (-7) \begin{vmatrix} 2 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp -9 \end{vmatrix}\\ \amp = 1(-18 - (-36)) - 4(36 - 0) - 7(-18 - 0)\\ \amp = 1(18) - 4(36) - 7(-18)\\ \amp = 18 - 144 + 126 = 0 \end{align*}
    Because the scalar triple product is exactly \(0\text{,}\) the bounded volume vanishes. This proves that the three vectors are coplanar.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.79.

    The product \(\vec{a} \times (\vec{b} \times \vec{c})\) is called the vector triple product of the vectors \(\vec{a}\text{,}\) \(\vec{b}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{c}\text{.}\) It can be expanded and computed using dot products via the BAC-CAB identity:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} \times (\vec{b} \times \vec{c}) = (\vec{a} \cdot \vec{c})\vec{b} - (\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b})\vec{c} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.80.

    Given the vectors \(\vec{a} = \vec{i} - \vec{j} + 2\vec{k}\text{,}\) \(\vec{b} = 2\vec{i} + \vec{k}\text{,}\) and \(\vec{c} = \vec{j} - 3\vec{k}\text{,}\) evaluate the vector triple product \(\vec{a} \times (\vec{b} \times \vec{c})\) using the expansion identity.
    🔗
    Solution.
    First, express the vectors cleanly in their numerical component forms:
    \begin{equation*} \vec{a} = \langle 1, -1, 2 \rangle, \quad \vec{b} = \langle 2, 0, 1 \rangle, \quad \vec{c} = \langle 0, 1, -3 \rangle \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Next, calculate the two scalar dot products required by the identity (\(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{c}\) and \(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}\)):
    \begin{align*} \vec{a} \cdot \vec{c} \amp = (1)(0) + (-1)(1) + (2)(-3) = 0 - 1 - 6 = -7\\ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} \amp = (1)(2) + (-1)(0) + (2)(1) = 2 + 0 + 2 = 4 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Substitute these scalar values back into the vector triple product identity formula:
    \begin{align*} \vec{a} \times (\vec{b} \times \vec{c}) \amp = (\vec{a} \cdot \vec{c})\vec{b} - (\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b})\vec{c} \\ \amp = -7\langle 2, 0, 1 \rangle - 4\langle 0, 1, -3 \rangle\\ \amp = \langle -14, 0, -7 \rangle - \langle 0, 4, -12 \rangle\\ \amp = \langle -14 - 0, \, 0 - 4, \, -7 - (-12) \rangle = \langle -14, -4, 5 \rangle \end{align*}
    In standard basis notation, the final vector is \(-14\vec{i} - 4\vec{j} + 5\vec{k}\text{.}\)
