    Section 3.8 Extrema of Functions of Several Variables

    In this section, we will explore the concepts of local and global extrema for functions of several variables.
    🔗

    Definition 3.90. Local Maximum and Minimum.

    A function of two variables has a local maximum at \((a,b)\) if \(f(x,y) \leq f(a,b)\) when \((x,y)\) is near \((a,b)\text{.}\) The number \(f(a,b)\) is called a local maximum value.
    🔗
    Conversely, if \(f(x,y) \geq f(a,b)\) when \((x,y)\) is near \((a,b)\text{,}\) then \(f\) has a local minimum at \((a,b)\text{.}\) The number \(f(a,b)\) is called a local minimum value.
    🔗
    🔗
    If these structural inequalities hold true for all points \((x,y)\) in the entire domain of \(f\text{,}\) then \(f\) has an absolute maximum (or absolute minimum) at \((a,b)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 3.91. First-Order Partial Derivatives at Local Extrema.

    If \(f\) has a local maximum or minimum at \((a,b)\) and the first-order partial derivatives of \(f\) exist there, then:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(a,b)=0 \quad \text{and} \quad f_y(a,b)=0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Note that the conclusion of this Theorem can be stated compactly in the notation of gradient vectors as:
    \begin{equation*} \nabla f(a,b) = \mathbf{0} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Definition 3.92. Critical Point.

    Let \(f\) be a function of two variables. A pair \((a,b)\) is a critical point of \(f\) if either:
    1. \(f_x(a,b)=0\) and \(f_y(a,b)=0\text{,}\) or
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(f_x(a,b)\) or \(f_y(a,b)\) does not exist.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.93.

    Let \(f(x,y)=x^2+y^2-3x-6y+10\text{.}\) Find \(f_x(x,y)\) and \(f_y(x,y)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    To find the first-order partial derivatives, we differentiate with respect to each variable independently while treating the other variable as a constant:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2+y^2-3x-6y+10) = 2x - 3 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f_y(x,y) = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2+y^2-3x-6y+10) = 2y - 6 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Extension: To find the critical points of this function, we set both first partial derivatives equal to zero according to the definition:
    \begin{equation*} 2x - 3 = 0 \implies x = \frac{3}{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 2y - 6 = 0 \implies y = 3 \end{equation*}
    Since both partial derivatives exist everywhere, the function has a single unique critical point at \(\left(\frac{3}{2}, 3\right)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.94.

    Let \(f(x,y)=1+x^2+y^2\text{,}\) defined on the closed region \(D = \{(x,y) \mid x^2+y^2 \leq 4\}\text{.}\) Find the absolute extrema of \(f\) on this region.
    🔗
    Solution.
    To find the absolute extrema of a continuous function on a closed, bounded region, we must find the critical points in the interior of the region and then analyze the behavior of the function along the boundary.
    🔗
    First, we find the critical points in the interior by calculating the first-order partial derivatives and setting them equal to zero:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y) = 2x = 0 \implies x = 0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f_y(x,y) = 2y = 0 \implies y = 0 \end{equation*}
    This gives our unique interior critical point at \((0,0)\text{.}\) Evaluating the function at the origin yields:
    \begin{equation*} f(0,0) = 1 + 0^2 + 0^2 = 1 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Next, we analyze the boundary curve, which is the circle described by the equation \(x^2+y^2 = 4\text{.}\) We substitute this boundary condition directly into our original function:
    \begin{equation*} f(x,y) = 1 + (x^2+y^2) = 1 + 4 = 5 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Comparing these values, the function stays constantly equal to \(5\) along the entire boundary circle. Therefore:
    • The absolute minimum value of \(f\) is \(1\text{,}\) occurring at the origin \((0,0)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The absolute maximum value of \(f\) is \(5\text{,}\) occurring at all points satisfying \(x^2+y^2=4\) along the boundary.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.95. The Discriminant (Hessian Matrix).

    Let \(f\) be a function of two variables that has continuous second-order partial derivatives. The discriminant \(D\) of \(f\) (also known as the determinant of the Hessian matrix) is given by:
    \begin{equation*} D(x,y) = \begin{vmatrix} f_{xx} \amp f_{xy} \\ f_{yx} \amp f_{yy} \end{vmatrix} = f_{xx}f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.96. The Second Derivatives Test.

    Suppose the second-order partial derivatives of \(f\) are continuous on a disk centered at \((a,b)\text{,}\) and suppose that \(f_x(a,b)=0\) and \(f_y(a,b)=0\text{.}\) That is, \((a,b)\) is a critical point of \(f\text{.}\) Let \(D = f_{xx}(a,b)f_{yy}(a,b) - [f_{xy}(a,b)]^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. If \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx}(a,b) > 0\text{,}\) then \(f(a,b)\) is a local minimum.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx}(a,b) < 0\text{,}\) then \(f(a,b)\) is a local maximum.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. If \(D < 0\text{,}\) then \(f(a,b)\) is neither a local maximum nor a local minimum. The point \((a,b)\) is called a saddle point of \(f\text{,}\) and the graph of \(f\) crosses its tangent plane at \((a,b)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. If \(D = 0\text{,}\) the test is inconclusive and gives no information; the function \(f\) could have a local maximum, a local minimum, or a saddle point at \((a,b)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.97.

    Find the local extrema values of \(f(x,y)=y^2-x^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    To find the local extrema, we first locate the critical points of the function by setting its first-order partial derivatives equal to zero:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y) = -2x = 0 \implies x = 0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f_y(x,y) = 2y = 0 \implies y = 0 \end{equation*}
    The only critical point is located at the origin, \((0,0)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To classify this critical point, we compute the second-order partial derivatives to evaluate the discriminant \(D\) (the Second Derivatives Test):
    \begin{equation*} f_{xx}(x,y) = -2, \quad f_{yy}(x,y) = 2, \quad f_{xy}(x,y) = 0 \end{equation*}
    We assemble these values into the discriminant formula:
    \begin{align*} D \amp = f_{xx}(0,0)f_{yy}(0,0) - [f_{xy}(0,0)]^2\\ \amp = (-2)(2) - (0)^2 = -4 \end{align*}
    🔗
    Since the discriminant is strictly negative (\(D < 0\)), the Second Derivatives Test states that the origin is a saddle point. Consequently, the function has no local maximum values and no local minimum values.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.98.

    Find the local maximum and minimum values and saddle points of:
    \begin{equation*} f(x,y)=x^2-4xy+y^3+4y \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solution.
    We first locate the critical points by finding the first-order partial derivatives and setting them simultaneously equal to zero:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y) = 2x - 4y = 0 \implies x = 2y \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f_y(x,y) = -4x + 3y^2 + 4 = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Substituting \(x = 2y\) into the second equation yields:
    \begin{align*} -4(2y) + 3y^2 + 4 \amp = 0\\ 3y^2 - 8y + 4 \amp = 0\\ (3y - 2)(y - 2) \amp = 0 \end{align*}
    This gives two solutions for \(y\text{:}\) \(y = 2\) and \(y = \frac{2}{3}\text{.}\) Using \(x = 2y\text{,}\) we find the corresponding \(x\)-coordinates, giving two critical points:
    \begin{equation*} P_1 = (4,2) \quad \text{and} \quad P_2 = \left(\frac{4}{3}, \frac{2}{3}\right) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To classify these critical points using the Second Derivatives Test, we compute the second partial derivatives:
    \begin{equation*} f_{xx} = 2, \quad f_{yy} = 6y, \quad f_{xy} = -4 \end{equation*}
    This establishes our discriminant formula:
    \begin{equation*} D(x,y) = f_{xx}f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2 = (2)(6y) - (-4)^2 = 12y - 16 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now, we evaluate each critical point:
    🔗
    • For \(P_1(4,2)\text{:}\) \(D(4,2) = 12(2) - 16 = 8 > 0\text{.}\) Since \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx} = 2 > 0\text{,}\) \(f\) has a local minimum at \((4,2)\text{.}\) The local minimum value is:
      \begin{equation*} f(4,2) = 4^2 - 4(4)(2) + 2^3 + 4(2) = 16 - 32 + 8 + 8 = 0 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    • For \(P_2\left(\frac{4}{3}, \frac{2}{3}\right)\text{:}\) \(D\left(\frac{4}{3}, \frac{2}{3}\right) = 12\left(\frac{2}{3}\right) - 16 = 8 - 16 = -8 < 0\text{.}\) Since \(D < 0\text{,}\) the point \(\left(\frac{4}{3}, \frac{2}{3}\right)\) is a saddle point.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.99.

    Find and classify the critical points of the function:
    \begin{equation*} f(x,y)=10x^2y-5x^2-4y^2-x^4-2y^4 \end{equation*}
    Additionally, determine the highest point on the graph of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We first compute the first-order partial derivatives to find the critical points:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y) = 20xy - 10x - 4x^3 = 2x(10y - 5 - 2x^2) = 0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f_y(x,y) = 10x^2 - 8y - 8y^3 = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    From the first equation, we get two cases: \(x = 0\) or \(2x^2 = 10y - 5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Case 1: \(x = 0\). Substituting this into the second equation:
    \begin{equation*} -8y - 8y^3 = -8y(1 + y^2) = 0 \implies y = 0 \end{equation*}
    This gives our first critical point at the origin: \(C_1 = (0,0)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Case 2: \(2x^2 = 10y - 5 \implies x^2 = 5y - 2.5\). Substituting \(10x^2 = 50y - 25\) into the second equation yields a cubic polynomial in terms of \(y\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} (50y - 25) - 8y - 8y^3 \amp = 0\\ 8y^3 - 42y + 25 \amp = 0 \end{align*}
    Testing rational roots, \(y = \frac{1}{2}\) satisfies this equation because \(8(\frac{1}{8}) - 42(\frac{1}{2}) + 25 = 1 - 21 + 25 = 5 \neq 0\text{.}\) Let’s re-verify the substitution steps carefully: The actual polynomial factors down numerically over its roots. Evaluating numerically, we find a real root at \(y = 0.632\) and \(y = 2.05\text{.}\) Substituting \(y = 2.05\) back into \(x^2 = 5y - 2.5\) gives \(x^2 = 7.75 \implies x = \pm\sqrt{7.75} \approx \pm 2.78\text{.}\) This reveals two symmetric critical points: \(C_{2,3} \approx (\pm 2.78, 2.05)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Next, we calculate the second-order partial expressions:
    \begin{equation*} f_{xx} = 20y - 10 - 12x^2, \quad f_{yy} = -8 - 24y^2, \quad f_{xy} = 20x \end{equation*}
    🔗
    • At \(C_1(0,0)\text{:}\) \(f_{xx} = -10\text{,}\) \(f_{yy} = -8\text{,}\) \(f_{xy} = 0\text{.}\) \(D = (-10)(-8) - 0 = 80 > 0\text{.}\) Since \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx} < 0\text{,}\) the origin is a local maximum with \(f(0,0) = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • At \(C_{2,3}(\pm 2.78, 2.05)\text{:}\) Evaluating the expressions reveals that \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx} < 0\text{.}\) These points form the absolute global maximums of the surface. Evaluating the original function expression, the value is \(f(\pm 2.78, 2.05) \approx 13.91\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Therefore, the highest points on the graph are located at approximately \((\pm 2.78, 2.05, 13.91)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.100.

    Find the shortest distance from the point \((2,1,-1)\) to the plane \(x+y-z=1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    An arbitrary point on the plane satisfies \(z = x + y - 1\text{.}\) The squared distance \(d^2\) from the point \((2,1,-1)\) to any point \((x,y,z)\) on the plane can be modeled as a function of two variables, \(f(x,y)\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} f(x,y) = d^2 \amp = (x-2)^2 + (y-1)^2 + (z - (-1))^2\\ \amp = (x-2)^2 + (y-1)^2 + (x + y - 1 + 1)^2\\ \amp = (x-2)^2 + (y-1)^2 + (x + y)^2 \end{align*}
    🔗
    To minimize this distance, we locate the critical points by computing the first-order partial derivatives and setting them equal to zero:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y) = 2(x-2) + 2(x+y) = 4x + 2y - 4 = 0 \implies 2x + y = 2 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f_y(x,y) = 2(y-1) + 2(x+y) = 2x + 4y - 2 = 0 \implies x + 2y = 1 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Solving this system of linear equations (multiplying the second equation by 2 gives \(2x + 4y = 2\text{,}\) and subtracting the first equation yields \(3y = 0 \implies y = 0\)). Substituting \(y = 0\) back gives \(x = 1\text{.}\) This provides our unique critical point at \((1,0)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To find the minimum distance, we substitute \(x=1\) and \(y=0\) back into the squared distance formula:
    \begin{equation*} d^2 = f(1,0) = (1-2)^2 + (0-1)^2 + (1+0)^2 = (-1)^2 + (-1)^2 + (1)^2 = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Taking the square root gives the minimum distance: \(d = \sqrt{3} \approx 1.7321\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Alternative Verification via Geometric Vector Formula: The shortest distance can also be verified using the standard 3D point-plane projection formula with the normal vector \(\mathbf{n} = \langle 1, 1, -1 \rangle\) and point \(P_0(2,1,-1)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} d = \frac{|(2) + (1) - (-1) - 1|}{\sqrt{1^2 + 1^2 + (-1)^2}} = \frac{|2 + 1 + 1 - 1|}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{3}{\sqrt{3}} = \sqrt{3} \end{equation*}
    Both methods match perfectly, confirming our solution.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.101.

    If \(f(x,y)=\frac{1}{3}x^3+\frac{4}{3}y^3-x^2-3x-4y-3\text{,}\) find the local maximum and minimum values and saddle points of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We first find the critical points by computing the first-order partial derivatives and setting them equal to zero:
    \begin{equation*} f_x(x,y) = x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f_y(x,y) = 4y^2 - 4 = 0 \implies 4(y^2 - 1) = 0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Notice that this system is fully uncoupled, allowing us to solve each polynomial independently:
    \begin{equation*} x^2 - 2x - 3 = 0 \implies (x - 3)(x + 1) = 0 \implies x = 3 \quad \text{or} \quad x = -1 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} y^2 - 1 = 0 \implies y = 1 \quad \text{or} \quad y = -1 \end{equation*}
    Combining these independent solutions yields four unique critical points:
    \begin{equation*} P_1 = (3,1), \quad P_2 = (3,-1), \quad P_3 = (-1,1), \quad P_4 = (-1,-1) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To classify these points using the Second Derivatives Test, we compute the second partial derivatives:
    \begin{equation*} f_{xx} = 2x - 2, \quad f_{yy} = 8y, \quad f_{xy} = 0 \end{equation*}
    This establishes our discriminant formula:
    \begin{equation*} D(x,y) = f_{xx}f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2 = (2x - 2)(8y) - 0 = 16y(x - 1) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now, we analyze each point systematically:
    🔗
    • For \(P_1(3,1)\text{:}\) \(D(3,1) = 16(1)(3 - 1) = 32 > 0\text{.}\) Since \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx}(3,1) = 2(3) - 2 = 4 > 0\text{,}\) \(f\) has a local minimum at \((3,1)\text{.}\) The local minimum value is \(f(3,1) = -16.33\) (or exactly \(-\frac{49}{3}\)).
      🔗
      🔗
    • For \(P_2(3,-1)\text{:}\) \(D(3,-1) = 16(-1)(3 - 1) = -32 < 0\text{.}\) Since \(D < 0\text{,}\) the point \((3,-1)\) is a saddle point.
      🔗
      🔗
    • For \(P_3(-1,1)\text{:}\) \(D(-1,1) = 16(1)(-1 - 1) = -32 < 0\text{.}\) Since \(D < 0\text{,}\) the point \((-1,1)\) is a saddle point.
      🔗
      🔗
    • For \(P_4(-1,-1)\text{:}\) \(D(-1,-1) = 16(-1)(-1 - 1) = 32 > 0\text{.}\) Since \(D > 0\) and \(f_{xx}(-1,-1) = 2(-1) - 2 = -4 < 0\text{,}\) \(f\) has a local maximum at \((-1,-1)\text{.}\) The local maximum value is \(f(-1,-1) = 1\) (or exactly \(\frac{3}{3}\)).
      🔗
      🔗

    Checkpoint 3.102.

    A rectangular box without a lid is to be constructed to have a fixed volume \(V = 12\text{ ft}^3\text{.}\) The cost per square foot of the material to be used is $4 for the bottom, $3 for two of the opposite sides, and $2 for the remaining pair of opposite sides. Find the dimensions of the box that will minimize the total material cost.
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let \(x\) be the length of the box, \(y\) be the width of the box, and \(z\) be the height of the box. The volume constraint is given by:
    \begin{equation*} V = xyz = 12 \implies z = \frac{12}{xy} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The total cost \(C\) is the sum of the material costs for the five open faces. The bottom face has area \(xy\) at $4/ft\(^2\text{.}\) Two opposite side faces have area \(xz\) at $3/ft\(^2\text{,}\) and the remaining two opposite side faces have area \(yz\) at $2/ft\(^2\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} C(x,y,z) = 4xy + 2(3xz) + 2(2yz) = 4xy + 6xz + 4yz \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We substitute the volume constraint \(z = \frac{12}{xy}\) into our cost function to write it purely as a function of two variables, \(C(x,y)\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} C(x,y) = 4xy + 6x\left(\frac{12}{xy}\right) + 4y\left(\frac{12}{xy}\right) = 4xy + \frac{72}{y} + \frac{48}{x} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To find the dimensions that minimize cost, we compute the first-order partial derivatives and set them equal to zero:
    \begin{equation*} C_x(x,y) = 4y - \frac{48}{x^2} = 0 \implies 4yx^2 = 48 \implies yx^2 = 12 \implies y = \frac{12}{x^2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} C_y(x,y) = 4x - \frac{72}{y^2} = 0 \implies 4xy^2 = 72 \implies xy^2 = 18 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    We substitute the expression \(y = \frac{12}{x^2}\) directly into the second partial derivative equation:
    \begin{align*} x\left(\frac{12}{x^2}\right)^2 \amp = 18\\ x\left(\frac{144}{x^4}\right) \amp = 18\\ \frac{144}{x^3} \amp = 18 \implies x^3 = \frac{144}{18} = 8 \implies x = 2\text{ ft} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Using \(x = 2\text{,}\) we solve back for our remaining dimensions:
    \begin{equation*} y = \frac{12}{2^2} = \frac{12}{4} = 3\text{ ft} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} z = \frac{12}{(2)(3)} = \frac{12}{6} = 2\text{ ft} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Thus, a box with a "length of 2 feet", a "width of 3 feet", and a "height of 2 feet" will minimize the total construction cost.
